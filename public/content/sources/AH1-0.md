Ibn Khaldun wrote the Muqaddimah ("Introduction" or "Prolegomena") in 1377 as the opening volume of a much larger world history, Kitab al-Ibar. It is widely regarded — including by many historians of science — as the first systematic attempt to analyze history through social, economic, and political causation rather than as a sequence of individual rulers and battles, which is why it's often cited as a founding text of sociology and historiography centuries before either field formally existed.

## Asabiyyah: the core concept

Ibn Khaldun's central analytical tool is asabiyyah — usually translated as "group solidarity," "social cohesion," or "esprit de corps." It is the bond of mutual loyalty and shared identity that lets a group act collectively, endure hardship, and subordinate individual interest to a common cause. He argued this bond is strongest among people living without luxury — nomadic or tribal groups shaped by harsh environments — precisely because survival under hardship forces genuine mutual reliance.

## The cycle

Ibn Khaldun's theory of history follows a repeating pattern:

1. A group with strong asabiyyah, typically from a harsh or marginal environment, unites around a shared cause or leader.
2. That cohesion gives them the discipline and unity to challenge and eventually conquer an established, urbanized dynasty that has grown "soft" from generations of comfort.
3. Having won power, the new rulers settle into urban life, accumulate wealth, and shift from communal loyalty toward individual privilege.
4. Over roughly three to four generations, asabiyyah erodes as the founding hardship recedes from living memory and is replaced by administrative bureaucracy, luxury, and internal rivalry.
5. The now-softened dynasty becomes vulnerable to the next outside group that still has strong asabiyyah — and the cycle repeats.

## Why it still gets cited

The theory has been applied well beyond its original medieval North African/Islamic context — to the rise and fall of the Mughal Empire, to modern political science discussions of state formation, and to general theories of institutional decay. Its central insight — that the very success and comfort a group achieves tends to dissolve the cohesion that produced that success — has made it a recurring reference point in discussions of why empires, companies, and movements decline from the inside rather than only from external defeat.
