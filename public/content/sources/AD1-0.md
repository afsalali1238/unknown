Donald Hebb introduced this theory of learning in his 1949 book The Organization of Behavior, decades before any imaging technology could observe synapses changing in real time. His actual formulation is more precise than the popular slogan it inspired:

> "Let us assume that the persistence or repetition of a reverberatory activity (or 'trace') tends to induce lasting cellular changes that add to its stability... When an axon of cell A is near enough to excite a cell B and repeatedly or persistently takes part in firing it, some growth process or metabolic change takes place in one or both cells such that A's efficiency, as one of the cells firing B, is increased."

## "Fire together, wire together" — and its fine print

The four-word slogan is often credited to neuroscientist Carla Shatz, who popularized it around 1992, not to Hebb himself. Hebb's actual claim is more specific than simultaneous firing: cell A has to help fire cell B — meaning A fires just _before_ B, not at the same time — for the connection to strengthen. This causal, timing-sensitive detail anticipated what neuroscience later formalized as spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP).

## What it explains

Hebbian learning is now the accepted cellular basis for associative learning and memory formation — when two neurons are repeatedly co-active in the right temporal order, the synapse between them physically strengthens (more receptors, larger synaptic contacts), making future co-activation easier. Nobel laureate Eric Kandel's experiments on the sea slug Aplysia californica provided some of the clearest direct evidence, since its large, accessible peripheral synapses are far easier to manipulate experimentally than mammalian brain tissue.

## Limits of the theory

Hebb's rule, taken in its purest mathematical form, is actually unstable — a purely Hebbian network with no counterbalancing mechanism would see weights grow without bound as strong connections keep reinforcing themselves. Real neural circuits avoid this runaway effect through inhibitory neurons and homeostatic mechanisms Hebb did not originally account for, which is why modern models pair Hebbian learning with normalization rules (like Oja's rule or BCM theory). Hebbian dynamics have also been proposed as the mechanism behind mirror neurons: repeated overlap between performing an action and perceiving it (hearing your own babbling, watching your own hand) wires perception and motor circuits together over time.
