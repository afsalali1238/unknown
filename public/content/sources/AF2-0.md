> Originally conducted by Stanley Milgram, 1967; this note summarizes https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small-world_experiment
> Archived 2026-07-17 for personal offline reading.

Milgram, then at Harvard, designed a deceptively simple field experiment: recruit a set of starting participants in Nebraska and Kansas, hand each of them a folder addressed to a specific target person — a stockbroker in Boston — and ask them to send it to that target using only people they know personally on a first-name basis. If they didn't know the target directly, they were to send it to someone they judged more likely to know the target, or know someone who would.

## What actually happened

Of the folders that eventually reached the target, the average number of intermediary hops was about 4.4. Milgram rounded and popularized this as "six degrees of separation" in later public accounts, though that exact phrase doesn't appear in his original papers (it's more associated with a later play and film by that name). It's also worth noting the experiment's limitations: most folders never reached the target at all, and the study measured only the successful chains, which likely biases the reported average downward compared to the true typical distance across the full population.

## Why the result mattered

Before Milgram, there was no strong empirical evidence for how "close" strangers actually are in a large population, despite intuitions on both sides — some expected social networks to be vast, sparse, and disconnected across social classes and geography; Milgram's result suggested the opposite. The explanation that later network scientists worked out mathematically is that most social networks are "small world" networks: densely clustered locally (your friends tend to know each other) but stitched together globally by a relatively small number of long-range, weak-tie connections that dramatically shrink the effective distance between any two people.

## Legacy

The concept underlies modern network science (formalized decades later by Watts and Strogatz's 1998 small-world network model), and it's become a standard mental model in business and career strategy: the value of a network often comes less from its total size than from a handful of well-placed bridge connections that link otherwise separate clusters.
