# Warren Buffett's Buy American Report Card

On October 16, 2008 — with the S&P 500 closing just under 908, in the depths of the post-Lehman panic — Warren Buffett published an op-ed in the New York Times titled "Buy American, I Am," urging readers to buy U.S. equities. He disclosed he was doing exactly that in his personal account, which had previously been fully allocated to Treasuries. Within five months the S&P fell further, bottoming near 666 in March 2009, before beginning the recovery that eventually carried it well above 1,200.

Critics noted that Buffett's public "Buy American" stance coincided with Berkshire Hathaway striking extraordinarily favorable private deals: preferred-share investments in Goldman Sachs and General Electric at terms far better than any retail investor reading the op-ed could access, plus the acquisition of railroad operator Burlington Northern. In Goldman's case, Berkshire's $5 billion investment came with warrants exercisable at a $115 strike price within five years, on top of a 10% dividend on the preferred shares — a position that was comfortably in the money as Goldman's share price recovered into the $130 range.

Still, even an ordinary investor who simply bought the S&P 500 index in October 2008 was up roughly 11% plus dividends by mid-2010 — a solid return given how dire conditions looked at the time Buffett wrote the piece, with Lehman's wreckage still being sorted out and AIG's bailout still an open question. The piece is frequently cited as a real-time example of Buffett's core discipline: buying when others are fearful, and being willing to look wrong for a long stretch before being proven right.
