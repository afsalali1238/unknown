I'm begging you to write more essays. Or to start writing essays if you aren't already. Not like the essays you were assigned in school — one of the greatest tools to learn faster, think deeper, improve articulation, and avoid being replaced by AI. But there's something deeper: essays might be one of the last forms of content that actually develops your capacity to make sense of reality.

## I – The internet isn't dead, but it's killing us

> The written word as the primary type of media was probably required for democracy to work, because it required the capacity to pay attention to an idea for long enough to understand it. — Daniel Schmachtenberger

The epistemic commons — our shared information water source — is becoming poisoned. Whenever content that hurts more than it helps isn't counterbalanced by content that helps, the intellectual water source becomes contaminated. Before you can solve any civilizational problem (climate, governance, AI alignment, public health), you need a population capable of understanding the problem coherently.

## II – The three forces breaking civilization's ability to think

Referencing Daniel Schmachtenberger's concept of the Metacrisis and its three "generator functions":

- **Rivalrous dynamics** — win-lose games (arms races, corporate competition, social media content, academic publishing).
- **Substrate consumption** — systems consuming their foundation faster than it regenerates (topsoil, attention).
- **Exponential technology** — tools improving themselves faster than human wisdom can keep up (AI, automated weapons, algorithms).

Converging, these lead to civilizational collapse or dystopian control. The "third attractor" — the good outcome — is a world of sense-making, shared understanding, and aligned incentives. When creators optimize for engagement over transformation, the substrate being consumed is cognitive capacity.

## III – Why essays may be the last bastion of real thinking

"Fast content" (listicles, rage tweets, AI summaries, engagement-optimized threads) delivers conclusions without requiring thought — fast food for the mind. "Slow content" (essays, long-form non-optimized conversation, certain books) requires thinking.

Distinguishing an article from an essay:

- Articles are answers, essays are arguments.
- Articles package existing knowledge, essays change the author's beliefs.
- Articles start with the conclusion, essays figure it out.
- Articles inform or educate, essays are an act of thinking.
- Articles communicate what's already there, essays discover what isn't.

The defining factor of an essay is that AI cannot write one — a robot doesn't have a situated point of view or direct experience. AI also destroys the magic of surprise and discovery: if you ask it to be novel, you're anticipating it, so it's no longer a surprise.

## IV – The meaning economy and how to thrive in it

Meaning is the scarcest commodity in civilization right now. Meaning is the experience of ordered consciousness: when attention is fragmented, that's psychic entropy (anxiety, boredom, restlessness); when attention is invested in a complex, challenging activity with clear feedback, that's psychic negentropy (flow, purpose, meaning).

Fast content skips the ordering process and delivers pre-packaged conclusions — the reader stays disordered, informed but empty. Slow content requires both writer and reader to engage in the ordering process. The opportunity: become a "value creator" — someone who chooses a positive trajectory for their life and documents the journey in public.

## V – How to think in public (and why it's the most valuable skill you can develop)

Practical guidance for writing essays:

- Write to discover, not to perform. Start with a concept, question, or something that bothers you.
- Write about what genuinely interests you. Focus on a single main idea. Go down rabbit holes.
- Resist the template — just write first, worry about structure later.
- Ask "Do I actually believe this?" — the point is to change what you believe, not confirm it.
- Read essays, consume "centropic" content — actively curate your inputs.
- Build a body of work, not a content calendar. Each essay builds on the last.

Platforms: X or Substack — the only two that prioritize long-form writing and thinking.
