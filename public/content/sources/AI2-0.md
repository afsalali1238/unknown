Stigler, a University of Chicago economist who would later win the 1982 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences, published this paper in 1971, directly challenging the then-dominant assumption in economics and public policy that government regulation exists primarily to correct market failures and protect the public interest.

## The capture model

Stigler proposed treating regulation itself as a good subject to ordinary economic forces of supply and demand: industries demand favorable regulation, and politicians and regulatory agencies supply it in exchange for political support. He argued that, as a general rule, regulation ends up being "acquired by the industry and designed and operated primarily for its benefit" rather than for consumers, because the industry being regulated is typically small in number, geographically concentrated, and highly motivated to organize and lobby, while the consumers who bear the cost of favorable regulation are numerous, dispersed, and only mildly affected as individuals.

## The mechanism

Because concentrated interests can overcome the coordination problem that prevents diffuse interests from organizing effectively (a related idea developed by economist Mancur Olson), regulatory outcomes systematically drift toward measures that protect incumbents from competition — licensing requirements, import tariffs, and other entry barriers — dressed in the language of consumer or public safety, even when their primary practical effect is to shield existing firms from new entrants.

## Legacy

Stigler's paper is credited with shifting mainstream economic analysis of regulation away from the "public interest" model and toward a "public choice" or "capture" model, a shift so thorough that within roughly a decade the earlier benign assumption about regulation had been largely abandoned in academic economics. The theory remains a standard reference point in debates over occupational licensing, financial regulation, and antitrust policy.
