# Persuasion

Persuasion is the influence people have on one another — changing someone's beliefs, decisions, or actions through reasoning or request rather than coercion. Marketers and advertisers draw heavily on a well-established set of psychological levers to make their pitches land.

The foundational set, drawn from Robert Cialdini's research, includes reciprocity (people feel obligated to return favors, which is why free samples and trial periods work), commitment and consistency (once someone takes a small public action, they feel pressure to act consistently with it going forward), social proof (people look to others' behavior, especially similar others, to decide what's correct — hence reviews, testimonials, and "bestseller" labels), authority (credentials, uniforms, and expert endorsements increase compliance), liking (we say yes more easily to people and brands we find likable or similar to ourselves), and scarcity (limited-time offers and low-stock warnings increase perceived value by threatening loss of access).

Beyond Cialdini's six, marketers also draw on behavioral-economics concepts: anchoring, where the first number or option a person sees skews their judgment of everything that follows; the decoy effect, where adding a deliberately unattractive third option makes a targeted middle option look like the obvious best choice; and framing, where identical information presented as a gain versus a loss produces very different decisions. Emotional appeals — fear and urgency to trigger survival-oriented quick decisions, or joy and hope to associate a product with a desired identity — round out the toolkit, alongside cognitive ease, the simple preference for messaging and design that requires the least mental effort to process.
