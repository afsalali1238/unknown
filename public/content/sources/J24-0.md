# What is First Principles Thinking?

First principles thinking means breaking a problem down into the elements you know are true, separating those from mere assumptions or conventions, and then rebuilding your reasoning from that irreducible foundation rather than reasoning by analogy to what's already been done. The piece uses a Lego-house metaphor: most people, handed an existing structure, just rearrange a few blocks to slightly improve it; first-principles thinkers take the whole thing apart into its individual pieces first, which opens the door to building something entirely different rather than a marginal variant.

Two concrete techniques for surfacing first principles are offered: Socratic questioning (a disciplined six-step process — clarify your thinking, challenge assumptions, look for evidence, consider alternative perspectives, examine consequences, and question the original question itself) and the "Five Whys," the child's game of repeatedly asking "why?" until you hit bedrock reasoning or discover you don't actually know the answer. Most adults find the second version of this uncomfortable in meetings, precisely because after two or three "whys" people are often confronted with the limits of their own understanding.

Elon Musk is the piece's central case study: rather than accepting the industry consensus that rockets and batteries are inherently expensive, Musk broke each down into raw material costs (aerospace-grade aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber for rockets; cobalt, nickel, aluminum for batteries) and found the material cost was a small fraction of market price — the "expense" was really an assumption inherited from how things had always been done, not a fact about the materials themselves. Other examples include Derek Sivers building CD Baby around the single first principle that a successful business needs happy customers (skipping funding, fancy offices, and staff in favor of obsessive customer care), and BuzzFeed's Jonah Peretti identifying wide distribution, not quality content per se, as the first principle of a successful website.

The piece closes by naming common limiting beliefs that first-principles thinking can dissolve — "I don't have a good memory," "there's too much information out there," "all the good ideas are taken," "we need to move first" (debunked with the observation that the iPhone wasn't first, just better) — arguing that most of these are inherited analogies rather than tested truths, and that the real cost of first-principles thinking is the mental effort of refusing to accept them uncritically.
