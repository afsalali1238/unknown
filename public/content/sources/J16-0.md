# The Pygmalion Effect: Proving Them Right

The Pygmalion effect is the psychological phenomenon where high expectations lead to improved performance, and its origin story runs through "Clever Hans," a horse that appeared able to do arithmetic by tapping his hoof. Investigation by psychologist Oskar Pfungst found Hans wasn't calculating at all — he was reading involuntary body-language cues from questioners who unconsciously signaled when he'd reached the right answer. Hans couldn't answer when blinkered or when the questioner didn't know the answer themselves. The case sparked decades of research into how expectations, transmitted through subtle unconscious cues, shape the performance of the people (and animals) around us.

The best-known demonstration is Robert Rosenthal and Lenore Jacobson's classroom study: teachers were told a random one-fifth of their students had tested as unusually gifted. By year's end, those randomly-labeled "gifted" students showed significantly larger IQ gains than their peers — not because they were actually different, but because teachers unconsciously gave them more attention, harder material, and more encouragement. The flip side is the Golem effect: low expectations produce measurably worse performance, through the same unconscious mechanism running in reverse.

The effect isn't limitless — J. Sterling Livingston's research on management found the Pygmalion effect only holds when the person is physically capable of what's expected of them, and research by McClelland and Atkinson found it drops off once someone perceives their odds of success as below 50%; expectations that feel completely out of reach can discourage rather than motivate. The practical lesson is to check your assumptions about people (the piece cites an exercise where students reveal starkly negative unconscious assumptions about people based on a one-line description) and to deliberately raise expectations within a believable range — the Tulane University case of janitor George Johnson being trained by teacher James Sweeney into a skilled computer operator, against colleagues' skepticism, is offered as a real-world illustration of the effect's power when applied intentionally.
