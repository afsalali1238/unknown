# Mental Model: Bias from Insensitivity to Sample Size

Humans intuitively understand that a sample of one person can't tell you the average height of everyone in the world, but that intuition breaks down for less extreme comparisons — most people don't reliably distinguish the reliability of a sample of 150 from a sample of 3,000, even though the difference matters enormously. Daniel Kahneman's framing in _Thinking, Fast and Slow_ is central here: our intuitive "System 1" constructs coherent stories from data without weighing how reliable that data actually is, so a compelling headline like "60% of surveyed seniors support the president" gets absorbed as a fact about seniors generally, with the sample size of 300 fading into background noise we never really process.

The canonical illustration is Kahneman and Tversky's hospital question: a town has a large hospital (45 births/day) and a small one (15 births/day); over a year, which hospital recorded more days where over 60% of babies born were boys? Most people guess "about the same," but the statistically correct answer is the smaller hospital — because smaller samples produce more extreme, more variable results purely by chance, a fact our intuition consistently underweights. The same logic explains why: a single poker session or six months of investment returns says almost nothing about someone's skill (luck dominates over short samples, and only accumulates into a reliable skill signal over thousands of hands or hundreds of trades); why "4 out of 5 doctors recommend..." ads are often built on a sample of five; and why early-season baseball standings routinely fail to predict where teams finish 162 games later.

The bias also distorts how organizations evaluate people. James March's "false record effect" describes how a group of managers of genuinely identical ability will show wide variation in short-run performance records purely from sampling noise — some will look outstanding, others ineffective, purely by chance, and that gap narrows the longer each manager stays in the role and the sample of their work grows. His companion "hero effect" is the mirror image: the faster someone is promoted within a group of managers of varying ability, the less likely that promotion is actually justified by ability rather than a lucky short sample. The broader takeaway is that sample-size insensitivity underlies both over- and under-estimation of risk, and undue confidence in patterns that are really just statistical noise — the fix isn't a formula, it's simply remembering to ask "how big was the sample?" before trusting a conclusion.
