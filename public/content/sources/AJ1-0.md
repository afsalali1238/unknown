Nash's paper, written while he was a 21-year-old Princeton graduate student, is famously just one page long. It thanks his advisor David Gale for suggesting the use of Kakutani's fixed-point theorem to simplify the proof, and was funded in part by the Atomic Energy Commission. Despite its brevity, it became one of the most cited results in 20th-century social science.

## What it proves

Nash defines an n-person game in which each player has a finite set of possible pure strategies, and a specific payoff corresponds to every combination of strategies chosen across all players. He then proves that every such finite game has at least one equilibrium point — a combination of strategies (possibly "mixed," meaning players randomize between options with certain probabilities) such that no single player can improve their own payoff by unilaterally switching strategy, given what everyone else is doing.

This was a generalization of earlier work: John von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern had proven equilibria existed for two-person zero-sum games (where one player's gain is exactly the other's loss). Nash's contribution extended the result to any number of players and to non-zero-sum games — where cooperation, partial alignment, and mutual loss are all possible — which is the vast majority of real strategic situations, from business competition to arms races to everyday negotiation.

## Stability, not optimality

The critical and often-missed nuance: a Nash equilibrium describes strategic stability, not collective good outcomes. The prisoner's dilemma is the canonical illustration — both players defecting is a Nash equilibrium (neither can improve by unilaterally switching to cooperation, since the other would then exploit them), even though mutual cooperation would leave both players better off. The equilibrium concept explains why groups can get durably stuck in outcomes that are individually unbeatable but collectively suboptimal.

## Legacy

The concept became the backbone of modern economics, auction design (including the U.S. government's spectrum auctions), evolutionary biology, and computer science (algorithmic game theory). Nash was awarded the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences in 1994, sharing it with John Harsanyi and Reinhard Selten for further developments in game theory.
