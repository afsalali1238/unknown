Manuscript received March 21, 1956.

**Abstract (as stated in the paper):** "If the input symbols to a communication channel represent the outcomes of a chance event on which bets are available at odds consistent with their probabilities (i.e., 'fair' odds), a gambler can use the knowledge given him by the received symbols to cause his money to grow exponentially. The maximum exponential rate of growth of the gambler's capital is equal to the rate of transmission of information over the channel. This result is generalized to include the case of arbitrary odds."

**The core setup — "the gambler with a private wire":** Kelly imagines a gambler who receives advance knowledge of the outcome of a chance event (e.g., the result of a baseball game) over a noisy communication channel, before that result becomes public, and can still bet at the original odds. If the channel were noiseless, the gambler would bet everything each time and his capital would double with every bet — pure exponential growth. The interesting case is a *noisy* channel, where the received signal is only probabilistically correct.

**The key result:** Kelly defines G, the exponential rate of growth of the gambler's capital, as the limit of (1/N) log(V_N / V_0), where V_N is capital after N bets. He shows that if the gambler bets a *fixed fraction* of his capital each time rather than staking everything, the fraction that maximizes G is not the fraction that maximizes expected winnings — betting everything each time maximizes expected value but leads to certain ruin over an indefinitely repeated game. Instead, the growth-maximizing fraction is set so that (1 + fraction) = 2q and (1 - fraction) = 2p, where p and q are the channel's error and correct-transmission probabilities. At this optimum, G_max exactly equals R, Shannon's channel capacity (the rate of transmission of information) — the paper's central and, at the time, novel finding: an information-theoretic quantity turns out to have a direct operational meaning in terms of a gambler's long-run capital growth, even with no coding involved.

**Generalization to arbitrary (non-fair) odds and the "track take" case:** Kelly extends the result to channels with multiple, unequally likely input symbols and to betting odds that are not the reciprocal of the true probabilities. He shows that under fair odds, the optimal strategy is for the gambler to bet in proportion to his true (posterior) probability of each outcome and to ignore the posted odds entirely. Any deviation of the odds from "fair" only helps the gambler. He also works out the more complex case where a "track take" (a bookmaker's cut) is present, deriving a maximizing procedure for capital allocation across bets.

**Conclusion (Kelly's own framing):** the gambler described in the paper follows a fundamentally different criterion from the "classical" gambler who simply maximizes expected winnings. Kelly's gambler instead maximizes the *expected value of the logarithm* of his capital at each bet — not because of any utility-function argument about diminishing returns to money, but purely because the logarithm is the quantity that is additive across repeated bets and to which the law of large numbers applies. Kelly notes explicitly that this reasoning requires an ongoing, repeated, reinvested game: a gambler permitted to bet a fixed one dollar each week without reinvesting winnings should instead simply maximize expectation on each individual bet. He closes by noting the model, while drawn from gambling, could apply "to certain other economic situations" wherever profits can be reinvested and bet sizing can be controlled — the "channel" of the theory corresponding either to a literal communication channel or simply to the totality of inside information available to an investor.

This 1956 paper is the origin of what later became known as the Kelly Criterion, the formula for optimal bet/position sizing that underlies modern quantitative bankroll and portfolio-sizing theory, and which Edward Thorp later adapted directly from Kelly's formula for both blackjack card-counting and hedge-fund position sizing (see the Edward O. Thorp source archived alongside this one).
