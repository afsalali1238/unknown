# Short-seller: Is Valeant the pharmaceutical Enron?

Valeant Pharmaceuticals' stock fell as much as 39% in a single day in October 2015, briefly halting trading, after a report by activist short-seller Andrew Left's Citron Research (which was actively shorting Valeant) revealed the company was using a specialty pharmacy, Philidor, to run "an entire network of phantom captive pharmacies" — allegedly booking sales of drugs that were, per Citron, effectively just sitting in inventory. Citron dubbed Valeant "the pharmaceutical Enron."

CEO J. Michael Pearson disclosed only briefly that Valeant had an option to acquire Philidor, and the company called Citron's claims "erroneous," saying sales were "recorded only when the product is dispensed to the patient." Specialty pharmacies like Philidor are exempt from reporting sales to IMS Health, the industry's standard drug-sales tracker — a structure originally meant to help distribute complex drugs (injections, refrigerated treatments) that instead, per analysts, was being used to push standard, higher-priced drugs insurers might otherwise decline to cover.

The Philidor revelations came amid broader scrutiny of Valeant's business model: buying "mispriced" older drugs and sharply raising their prices (in some cases documented increases of over 700%), and a federal subpoena demanding details of its pricing and distribution practices. Pearson said the company would pull back from the price-hike strategy and refocus on R&D.
