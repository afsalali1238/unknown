# Luckin, the scandal-tainted Chinese coffee upstart, plots an improbable comeback

In January 2020, Muddy Waters Research published an anonymous 89-page report — later attributed by the Wall Street Journal to Chinese hedge fund Snow Lake Capital — alleging Luckin Coffee had systematically inflated sales since its 2019 Nasdaq debut, based on over 1,500 investigators tracking sales and recording thousands of hours of video across 600+ retail outlets. Luckin initially denied the report, but a subsequent internal audit found its COO and subordinates had fabricated $337 million of 2019 sales — about a quarter of that year's business.

By mid-2020: Luckin's stock had plunged 90% (erasing $11 billion in market value), the company was delisted from Nasdaq, China's market regulator fined it $9 million, and the U.S. SEC fined it a further $180 million. Luckin filed for Chapter 15 bankruptcy in February 2021.

Yet less than two years later, under new CEO Jinyi Guo and backed by $240 million from private equity firm Centurium Capital, Luckin had overtaken Starbucks as China's largest coffee chain by store count (6,024 vs. 5,654 stores), narrowing its losses from $406 million (2020) to $86 million while revenue grew 80% year-over-year. It settled a $175 million class-action suit within eight months of filing for bankruptcy protection. Analysts noted Chinese consumers largely treated the fraud as an "accounting" problem rather than a food-safety or personal betrayal, unlike the reaction among Wall Street investors — helping the brand recover domestically even as its path back to U.S. exchanges remained uncertain.
