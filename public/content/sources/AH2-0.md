Gibbon published the first volume of this work in 1776, completing all six volumes by 1788. It covers roughly thirteen centuries, from the height of the Roman Empire under the "Five Good Emperors" in the 2nd century CE through the fall of Constantinople in 1453, and remains one of the most influential works of history ever written, both for its scope and its literary style.

## The central thesis

Gibbon's most famous and most contested claim is that Rome's fall was not primarily the result of external military defeat, but a long process of internal institutional and civic decay that had been underway for centuries before any barbarian army delivered a final blow. He pointed to several intertwined causes: a professional, increasingly foreign-recruited army with diminishing loyalty to Roman civic identity; an overextended and expensive imperial bureaucracy; the erosion of the civic virtue and political participation that had characterized the earlier Republic; and a governing elite increasingly focused on preserving personal status and luxury rather than effective administration.

## The controversial Christianity argument

Gibbon's most debated claim, laid out especially in chapters 15 and 16, was that the rise of Christianity contributed to Roman decline by redirecting the empire's civic and martial energy toward otherworldly concerns and internal theological disputes, and by fostering values (humility, unworldliness) that Gibbon saw as poorly suited to sustaining a militarized, expansionist state. This argument drew immediate and sustained criticism from religious authorities on publication and remains disputed by historians, many of whom see it as an overstatement of one factor among many.

## Why it's still read

Beyond the specific causal argument, the book established a template that later historians and analysts of institutional decline still use: distinguishing a proximate cause (the event that visibly triggers a collapse) from the deeper structural causes that made the system vulnerable to that event in the first place — a distinction commonly applied well outside Roman history, to the decline of companies, movements, and other institutions.
