# The Surprising Power of The Long Game

In everything we do, we play either the short game or the long game. The short game is easy, pleasurable, and offers visible, immediate benefits — but it almost never leads to lasting success. The long game is simpler to win, but not easier: it requires repeatedly doing hard things today that make tomorrow easier, and most people lack the patience for it. As Shane Parrish puts it, quoting a friend's saying, "If you do what everyone else is doing, you shouldn't be surprised to get the same results everyone else is getting."

The short game seduces with immediate, visible payoffs: spending more than you earn, skipping sleep, taking advantage of counterparties, neglecting relationships until you need them. Each of these feels fine in the moment, but the accumulation of tiny disadvantages compounds just as surely as tiny advantages do — only in the opposite direction. Eventually the costs become too large to ignore, and by then the short-game player has usually locked themselves into a worse position than when they started.

The long game looks boring from the outside — leaving a party early to sleep, eating well while others eat junk, investing in relationships before you need them, spending less than you make. None of this is debatable or mysterious; everyone already knows the formula for building wealth or strong relationships. What's scarce isn't the knowledge, it's the patience to act on it before the payoff is visible. The first step is the hardest, because it requires being willing to suffer a little today for a tomorrow you can't yet see the benefit of.

The piece closes with a practical framing: you can't play the long game in everything, so the highest-leverage places to apply it are things that compound — knowledge, relationships, and finances. Time amplifies the gap between long-game and short-game strategies; the earlier you start compounding in these domains, the larger the eventual gap between you and everyone still playing for today.
