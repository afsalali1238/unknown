# What Sharks Can Teach Us About Survivorship Bias

Most people name the great white as the deadliest shark to humans — a belief shaped heavily by *Jaws* and decades of pop-culture reinforcement. But great whites leave a lot of survivors: their fatality rate in documented attacks is only about 16%, lower than the bull shark's 21.5% or the tiger shark's 26%. Great whites simply generate three times as many attacks in total (mostly because they mistake humans for seals near shore), which means three times as many survivors around to tell their story — inflating the species' reputation relative to its actual lethality.

The sharks we should fear most are likely ones we almost never hear about. The oceanic white tip, a pelagic species that lives in the open ocean far from shore, has only fifteen documented attacks on record — but that's largely because pelagic encounters happen in isolated settings (a couple of people on a boat, not five hundred swimmers at a beach) where fewer people are present to survive and report an attack. The clearest evidence of how dangerous white tips can be comes from the 1945 sinking of the USS Indianapolis: of 1,195 crewmen aboard, only 316 survived days adrift in shark-infested open water, in what remains the worst single loss of life in US naval history — a data point that exists mainly because it involved enough people that some survived to describe it.

The generalizable lesson: our risk assessments are built almost entirely from survivor accounts, and the absence of survivors from a category doesn't mean that category is safe — it may mean we simply never hear from anyone who encountered it. The same distortion applies to "dangerous cities," career paths, and industries: fields with lots of publicly discussed failures (like tech startups) can seem riskier than fields where failures happen quietly and privately (like real estate or architecture), even when the underlying odds of failure are comparable or worse in the quiet field. Before drawing conclusions from what's visible, the piece argues, always ask: what happened to the people I'm not hearing from?
