# Bayes and Deadweight: Using Statistics to Eject the Deadweight From Your Life

Bayesian updating, at its core, is a simple discipline: start with what you already believe about the world, then revise incrementally as new information comes in — rather than either ignoring new evidence or overreacting to it as if it invalidates everything you knew before. The piece's practical trick for applying this to messy, subjective judgments (like evaluating a relationship) is to quantify your belief with an imagined bet: how much money would you actually wager, in a casino, on a specific prediction? If you wouldn't risk a dime on "this person's cat will doom the relationship," that tells you the information barely moves your probability estimate at all.

Applied to friendships and relationships, the method addresses a specific failure mode: treating all positive and negative behaviors as equally weighted, so a friend who "always remembers my birthday but also betrayed a confidence I asked her to keep" gets averaged into a vague, unhelpful overall impression rather than being properly weighted by what actually predicts future behavior. The Bayesian reframe asks a sharper question: given everything you know, how much would you bet that she does it again? That number — not a vague gut feeling — is what should drive whether the friendship is worth investing in.

The method's other benefit is that it interrupts the paralyzing spiral of "why did they do this?" — which tends to generate endless self-justifying rumination without changing anything. Instead of analyzing motive, the piece argues you should estimate recurrence probability, since that's the only number that actually determines what you should do next. For relationships you can't simply exit (family being the standard example), the same tool works in reverse: you can experiment with changing your own behavior and observe whether your internal "bet" on the outcome shifts, which tells you whether a given intervention is actually working before you commit to it long-term.
