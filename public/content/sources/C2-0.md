# Madoff Whistle-Blower Remembers: 'No One Would Listen'

The man who tried futilely for 10 years to expose the largest Ponzi scheme in history has written a book about his failed crusade: "No One Would Listen: A True Financial Thriller."

Harry Markopolos first suspected Bernard Madoff was a fraud in 1999, when a marketing colleague, Frank Casey, asked him to model Madoff's investment returns for a competing product. "He looks at it for five minutes I swear, and he says, this thing's a fraud," Casey recalled.

Markopolos, a math whiz working at the Boston firm Rampart Investments, assembled a small team — Casey, analyst Neil Chelo, and journalist Michael Ocrant — modeled on an anti-terrorist cell. He believed Madoff was either running an insider-trading scheme or a Ponzi scheme, and by 2002, after hearing money managers across Europe boast of a "special relationship" with Bernie, he concluded it was a Ponzi scheme: new investor money paying off old investors, with no real returns being generated at all.

Markopolos submitted detailed complaints to the SEC starting in 2000, culminating in a 2005 submission titled, in plain English, "The World's Largest Hedge Fund Is a Fraud," which laid out more than two dozen red flags. The SEC investigated "out of an overabundance of caution" and found no fraud. Markopolos believed Madoff was untouchable partly because of New York's deference to him as a former NASDAQ chairman and SEC advisor, and partly because his own team, working out of Boston, was dismissed as outsiders.

Madoff's scheme collapsed in December 2008 when it could no longer attract enough new money to pay existing investors, and he turned himself in, confessing to a $50 billion fraud — at the time the largest Ponzi scheme ever uncovered. Markopolos testified before Congress in February 2009 about what he saw as systemic failures at the SEC.

"I'm willing to stand up here and tell you that I failed," Markopolos said. "And it's $65 billion worth of failure."
