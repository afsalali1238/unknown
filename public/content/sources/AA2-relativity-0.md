Einstein submitted this paper on June 30, 1905, while working full-time as a patent examiner in Bern, with no university affiliation and little formal recognition in physics at the time. It is one of four landmark papers he published that year (his "miracle year"), and the one that introduced special relativity.

## The two postulates

Einstein built the entire theory from two starting assumptions: first, that the laws of physics take the same form in every inertial (non-accelerating) reference frame; second, that the speed of light in a vacuum is the same for every observer, regardless of the motion of the light source or the observer. Neither assumption looks radical in isolation — the surprise is what happens when you insist both hold simultaneously.

## The consequences that fall out

If light's speed can't change no matter how fast you're moving toward or away from its source, something else has to give — and Einstein showed it's space and time themselves. Moving clocks run slow relative to a stationary observer (time dilation); moving objects appear contracted along their direction of motion (length contraction); and simultaneity becomes observer-dependent — two events that happen "at the same time" for one observer can happen at different times for another observer in relative motion, with neither observer being objectively wrong. The famous mass-energy equivalence, E=mc², emerges later in the same theoretical framework as a further consequence.

## Why it mattered beyond physics

Special relativity ended the idea of a single universal "now" shared by everyone in the universe, replacing it with a structure where simultaneity, duration, and length are all relative to the observer's frame of motion. The paper used almost no new mathematics — it's built from careful logical reasoning about measurement and observation — which is part of why it's still taught as a model of what rigorously following an assumption to its full conclusion can produce.
