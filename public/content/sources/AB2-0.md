Hamilton wrote this pair of papers as a PhD student in London, publishing them in 1964. They address a problem Darwin himself flagged as a challenge to natural selection: sterile worker ants and bees appear to act against their own reproductive interest by helping the colony's queen reproduce instead of themselves. Pure individual-survival selection has no good explanation for this.

## The core mechanism

Hamilton's solution was to shift the unit of selection from the individual organism to the gene. A gene "cares" about its own propagation, and copies of a given gene exist not just in the individual carrying it but, with known probability, in that individual's relatives. Using J.B.S. Haldane's earlier intuition as a starting point, Hamilton built a rigorous mathematical model — incorporating Sewall Wright's coefficient of relationship — that formalized when a costly, self-sacrificing behavior would still be favored by selection.

## Hamilton's Rule

The condition is often written as rB > C: a behavior is favored when the benefit (B) to the relative, multiplied by the coefficient of relatedness (r) between actor and recipient, exceeds the cost (C) to the actor. A gene that pushes an individual to help a full sibling (r = 0.5) needs the benefit to that sibling to be more than double the cost to itself in order to spread — but that threshold is exactly what many social insects clear, given their unusual genetics (haplodiploidy), which pushes relatedness among sister workers even higher than 0.5 in some cases.

## Reception and legacy

The papers were initially obscure — dense, mathematical, and published by an unknown graduate student — but the concept of inclusive fitness eventually became so foundational to evolutionary biology that citing it explicitly is often considered unnecessary, the same way one wouldn't cite Newton for gravity. It revolutionized how biologists explain cooperation, altruism, and social structure across the animal kingdom, from insect colonies to primate kin networks.
