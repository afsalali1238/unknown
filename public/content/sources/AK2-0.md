Baudrillard, a French sociologist and philosopher, published this essay in 1981 as a central text of postmodern theory, examining how symbols, media, and representation function in advanced consumer and media societies.

## The four stages of the image

Baudrillard proposed that images and representations pass through four historical stages in their relationship to reality: first, the image is a faithful copy — a "good" reflection of a real thing; second, the image begins to distort or mask that underlying reality; third, the image masks the absence of any underlying reality at all, pretending to represent something that isn't really there; and fourth, the image becomes "pure simulacrum" — it bears no relation to any reality whatsoever, and simply generates its own reality through the play of signs referencing only other signs.

## Hyperreality

The term Baudrillard coined for this fourth stage is hyperreality: a condition in which the distinction between the real and its representation collapses entirely, and the simulation becomes, functionally, more emotionally and socially "real" than whatever it was originally meant to represent. His famous illustrative example is Disneyland, which he argued exists partly to make the rest of America (equally simulated, in his view) seem "real" by contrast — a simulation whose function is to conceal that the surrounding society is itself already simulated.

## Influence

The book became a touchstone across media studies, philosophy, and popular culture — most visibly as a direct visual and conceptual reference in the 1999 film The Matrix, whose creators have cited it explicitly. Its central warning — that a media environment can generate self-referential representations so complete and compelling that people lose any stable way to distinguish them from the underlying reality — has been repeatedly reapplied to describe social media, curated online identity, and algorithmically generated content well beyond the media landscape Baudrillard was originally writing about.
