Most skills will be irrelevant in 10-20 years — but a high-agency individual isn't dependent on any specific skill for success. Agency (not any particular skill) is the one thing that stays relevant now, in 10 years, and until you die, because it lets you set your own direction, avoid distraction, and adapt regardless of what gets automated.

## I – Agency is the ability to iterate without permission

> It is only those who are in constant revolt that discover what is true... — Krishnamurti

Conformity is a stage of cognitive development where truth is judged by popularity/acceptance rather than direct investigation — per Spiral Dynamics / stages of ego development, roughly half the population sits at the conformist stage. Conformity stems from survival: a job you're financially dependent on gives you low agency in that domain; hard-set religious/political identity gives you low agency there too. "This letter has a degree of conformity. We are all conformists in some way."

Three traits of high-agency people:

1. **They iterate without permission.** Agency is "not only action, but an undying commitment to iteration" — learning and doing together, correcting mistakes without retreating into conformity because "it's not working."
2. **They treat life as one giant experiment.** Low agency = "employee mindset" (given a task, decision-making compromised by craving tribal acceptance). High agency = scientist of your own life: form a hypothesis, test, expect to fail as part of the process, rather than blaming yourself or others when a promised path doesn't pan out.
3. **They believe in the difficult.** Goals come in three tiers — easy (already achievable), difficult (achievable after acquiring skills/resources), impossible (currently outside reality, or reachable only after clearing enough difficult goals). Referencing Seligman's learned-helplessness dog experiment: dogs previously shocked with no escape later didn't attempt to escape even when a simple exit was available — "you bear the shocks of the default path" because you were trained to believe there's no way out.

## II – AI is not a threat to the high agency

Access to knowledge has never been higher, yet most people still do nothing with it — proof that the bottleneck was never access, it was agency. High-agency people will outpace everyone else because barriers to action are nearly gone.

On AI and content: AI is a tool that still needs someone directing it toward a purpose. Asking AI to guess what will go viral produces content with no throughline, theme, personality, or vision — "creators are context creators, not content creators." 99% of AI-generated content underperforms because the value was never really "AI-generated," it was orchestrated by a human passing their own context into it. Building a business, writing a book, or making art with AI still requires you to hold the vision and control the details — "tools get replaced. Vision and agency do not."

## III – Why generalists win in the AI age

> Schools were created to enslave the brightest minds by promising the prestige of specialization... — Purpose & Profit

The famous "jack of all trades, master of none" quote is a misquote — the full line ends "but oftentimes better than a master of one." Shakespeare himself synthesized deep knowledge of human nature, language, classical literature, stagecraft, religion, philosophy, military tactics, music, and more. Visionaries (Darwin, Steve Jobs) have a specific vision they pursue — that's a vessel, not the same as being a narrow specialist; specialists are attached to a _skill_, and skills get disrupted by technology (Photoshop disrupted design; AI is repeating the pattern).

Humans are generalist tool-builders by nature — a lion in Alaska or a polar bear in the Savannah would die, but a human in either place builds shelter and finds food. The American public school system borrowed the Prussian model (mandatory attendance, teacher training, standardized testing, grade levels) originally designed to produce obedient, compliant workers — a system that "takes the shape of that which most benefits" those who built it, whether or not it's a deliberate conspiracy.

## IV – The 5 human capabilities

Addressing whether AGI makes human intelligence irrelevant, framed through 5 fundamental capabilities and whether AI can exceed humans at each:

1. **Computation (mental)** — bounded by processing/memory, but a universal computer (which we already have) can compute anything given time; AGI computing faster doesn't speed up the physical world.
2. **Transformation (physical)** — turning raw materials into things (rockets, tools); this is bound by time, not intelligence — "AGI may compute faster than our brains, but that doesn't mean it will create the thing any faster than humans."
3. **Variation** — generating new ideas within "idea space" (a map with explored "light" areas and unexplored "dark" potential); both humans and AGI are bound by the same laws of nature but neither has a hard ceiling here.
4. **Selection** — being able to identify which of many generated ideas are actually good, enabling cumulative (not restarted-from-scratch) progress; humans do this as "universal cybernetic systems" that error-correct, no fundamental disadvantage vs. AGI.
5. **Attention** — the ability to change perspective/lens when a problem arises (switching between a scientific lens and a spiritual one, for instance). Over-identifying with any single lens (including pure spirituality) makes for "an incomplete system that will fail to solve certain sets of problems."

Conclusion: AGI doesn't appear to surpass humans at any of these unless it "bends what is possible," in which case a very different problem exists anyway.

## V – How to actually practice agency

> ...in games, we can take up an end for the sake of the means. Playing games can be a motivational inversion of ordinary life. — C. Thi Nguyen, _Games: Agency As Art_

You develop your own agency by first practicing within other people's systems (games, courses, mentors) until you can create your own — "the most important high agency trait is to know when to break free." Games are framed as the art form that "records agencies" the way painting records sights and music records sounds — you start at level one, and the more you level up, the more you get to choose your own next challenge instead of having it assigned.

Practical steps:

1. Research processes others have found success with (YouTube, social media, courses, mentors).
2. Experiment with those techniques and try to get results (most won't work for you — expected).
3. Identify the patterns, principles, and levers that actually produced results.
4. Create your own process, tailored to your situation.
5. Pass it down to others — teaching forces you to actually understand it.

Social media is highlighted as a good vessel for this because it's low-cost/low-risk, lets you study others' agency directly in their content, gives immediate public feedback on what works, and forces a future-proof skill stack (writing, persuasion, marketing, sales, storytelling).
