# Falsification: How to Destroy Incorrect Ideas

Karl Popper argued that no scientific theory can be proven true with certainty — the only rigorous test is trying to prove it _false_, a process he called falsification. Humans, it turns out, are instinctively bad at this: it's far more mentally satisfying to search for evidence confirming a belief we already hold than to actively hunt for evidence that would destroy it, a pattern known as confirmation bias. Charlie Munger's line captures the mechanism well: "the human mind is a lot like the human egg... when one sperm gets in, it shuts down so the next one can't get in."

The piece's central illustration is psychologist Peter Cathcart Wason's 2-4-6 experiment: subjects are given the number sequence 2-4-6 and told it follows a hidden rule, then asked to guess the rule by proposing other three-number sequences and getting yes/no feedback. Most people's first instinct is "ascending even numbers," and they proceed to test sequences like 8-10-12 and 20-22-24 — both of which get confirmed, building false confidence — because subjects instinctively seek sequences that would _confirm_ their hypothesis rather than ones that would break it. The actual rule was simply "any ascending numbers" (so 1-2-3 or 4-23-512 would have both fit and immediately falsified the "even numbers" theory) — but because almost nobody tests sequences designed to fail, only about one in five participants ever guessed correctly.

The core lesson generalizes well beyond number puzzles: any time you're testing a belief, the diagnostic move isn't to look for confirming evidence (which is nearly always available if you look hard enough) but to actively seek the evidence most likely to prove you wrong. It's psychologically uncomfortable — deliberately going looking for disappointment — which is exactly why the discipline of falsification has to be practiced consciously rather than left to instinct.
