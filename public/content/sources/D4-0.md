# Waiting for the Last Dance

This piece summarizes a January 2021 note by Jeremy Grantham, co-founder of GMO and one of value investing's most prominent bears, arguing that the long U.S. bull market that began in 2009 had "finally matured into a fully-fledged epic bubble," comparable in scale to the South Sea Bubble, 1929, and 2000: "featuring extreme overvaluation, explosive price increases, frenzied issuance, and hysterically speculative investor behavior."

Grantham's evidence: speculative excess visible in individual names (Tesla's market cap, at the time, implied over $1.25 million per car sold annually versus about $9,000 for GM); a record 480 IPOs in 2020, including 248 SPACs, exceeding the dot-com-era record of 406 in 2000; 150 non-micro-cap companies more than tripling in a single year, three times the rate of any year in the prior decade; an 8x increase in small retail purchases of call options versus 2019, itself already an elevated year; and the "Buffett indicator" (total market cap to GDP) breaking its 2000 record high. He noted the unusual disconnect of a market trading in the top few percent of historical valuation while the real economy sat in the worst few percent — "completely without precedent."

Grantham's guess for the trigger that would burst the bubble was, ironically, the successful rollout of COVID-19 vaccines: once the pandemic's most pressing uncertainty resolved, he expected investors to look around, realize the underlying economy remained weak, notice that fiscal stimulus would soon be withdrawn, and reassess valuations accordingly. He closed by noting he remained comparatively bullish on emerging-market equities, which he judged near multi-decade relative lows against the U.S.
