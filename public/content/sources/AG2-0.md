> Originally introduced by James J. Gibson, 1966 (elaborated in The Ecological Approach to Visual Perception, 1979); this note summarizes https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affordance
> Archived 2026-07-17 for personal offline reading.

Gibson, a perceptual psychologist, introduced the concept of an affordance as part of his broader "ecological approach" to perception — a deliberate break from the dominant view that perception was a two-step process of first registering neutral sensory data, then applying reasoning or memory to interpret what it means.

## The core claim

Gibson argued that what we perceive is not a value-neutral description of an object's physical properties, but the possibilities for action the object or environment offers a specific perceiver, given that perceiver's own body and capabilities. A flat, rigid, knee-high surface affords sitting to an adult human — not because the human calculates this from separately perceived facts about flatness, rigidity, and height, but because the "sit-ability" is directly part of what's perceived. The same surface offers no such affordance to an ant, because affordances are always relative to the perceiver's own scale and capabilities, not an intrinsic property of the object alone.

## From perception theory to design

The term stayed largely within perceptual psychology until cognitive scientist and design theorist Don Norman popularized it for a broader audience in his 1988 book The Design of Everyday Things, applying it specifically to human-made objects and interfaces. Norman used affordances (and the related concept of "signifiers" — visible cues that communicate an affordance) to explain why some designs are self-explanatory and others aren't: a door handle shaped to be pulled communicates "pull" without a sign; a flat plate on a door communicates "push." When a design's affordance is unclear or contradicts its actual function — a "push" plate on a door that must be pulled — the resulting confusion is now sometimes called a "Norman door."

## Why it's still cited

The concept remains central to UX and industrial design because it reframes a design failure not as user error but as a failure to make the correct action perceptually obvious — pushing designers to make an object's function legible through its form, rather than relying on instructions or labels to compensate for an unclear design.
