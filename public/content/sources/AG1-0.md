> Originally coined by Louis Sullivan, "The Tall Office Building Artistically Considered," Lippincott's Monthly Magazine, March 1896; this note summarizes https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Form_follows_function
> Archived 2026-07-17 for personal offline reading.

Sullivan coined the maxim while grappling with a genuinely new design problem: the steel-framed skyscraper, which had no precedent in the architectural pattern books of the day. If the shape of a tall office building wasn't going to come from copying an old style, something else had to determine its form — and for Sullivan, that something was the building's purpose.

## The actual quote

Sullivan's original phrasing is more sweeping than the popular shorthand suggests:

> "Whether it be the sweeping eagle in his flight, or the open apple-blossom, the toiling work-horse, the blithe swan, the branching oak, the winding stream at its base, the drifting clouds, over all the coursing sun, form ever follows function, and this is the law... It is the pervading law of all things organic and inorganic, of all things physical and metaphysical, of all things human and all things superhuman, of all true manifestations of the head, of the heart, of the soul, that the life is recognizable in its expression, that form ever follows function."

He credited the underlying idea to his mentor John Edelmann and, later in life, traced its root to the Roman architect Vitruvius's ancient principle that structures must be solid, useful, and beautiful (firmness, commodity, delight). The maxim is also frequently misattributed to sculptor Horatio Greenough, whose earlier writing anticipated similar ideas but predates Sullivan's coinage.

## The gap between theory and practice

Despite writing the modernist movement's founding line, Sullivan himself never fully practiced it — his buildings, including the famous Carson, Pirie, Scott and Company Building in Chicago, are covered in lush cast-iron and terracotta Art Nouveau ornament. It was the generation after him — the Bauhaus, Adolf Loos ("Ornament and Crime," 1910), and Sullivan's own student Frank Lloyd Wright — who took the phrase and turned it into a genuine stripped-down design doctrine. Wright himself modified it further, to "form and function are one," arguing the two should be integrated rather than one dictating the other.

## Where it still applies

The principle now extends well past architecture — into industrial design (a screwdriver's shape converges toward one optimal form), software engineering (structure emerging from actual functional requirements, as in test-driven development and domain-driven design), and enterprise systems (architecture that should serve business process, not constrain it).
