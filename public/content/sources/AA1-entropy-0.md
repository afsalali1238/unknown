Entropy was named and mathematically defined by Rudolf Clausius in his 1865 treatise "On Different Forms of the Fundamental Equations of the Mechanical Theory of Heat." Clausius built the word from Greek en- ("in") and trope ("transformation" / "turning"), deliberately choosing a term that echoed "energy" since he saw the two concepts as closely related — entropy as a measure of the heat energy in a system no longer available to do work.

## The core claim

Clausius's central result, now the Second Law of Thermodynamics, is that in any isolated system, entropy can never decrease — it stays constant during a reversible process and strictly increases during an irreversible one. This gave physics something it previously lacked: a quantity that behaves asymmetrically in time. Nearly every other fundamental law (Newton's mechanics, Maxwell's electromagnetism) is time-symmetric — the equations work identically if you reverse the direction of time. Entropy is the exception, and that asymmetry is why physicists often call the Second Law "time's arrow."

## Why disorder wins

The statistical explanation, developed later by Ludwig Boltzmann, is that entropy increase isn't a mysterious force pushing systems toward disorder — it's simple probability. There are vastly more ways for a system's particles to be arranged in a disordered configuration than an ordered one, so as a system evolves, it is statistically overwhelmingly likely to drift toward the disordered macrostates, not because ordered ones are forbidden, but because there are so few of them relative to the total possibility space. A shuffled deck of cards essentially never spontaneously sorts itself, not because sorting violates physics, but because there's only one sorted order and 52! roughly-disordered ones.

## Practical consequence

Every engine, biological organism, and star is, in thermodynamic terms, a local, temporary entropy-decreasing structure that is only possible because it exports even more entropy to its surroundings — life and machines don't violate the Second Law, they pay for local order with a bigger disorder bill elsewhere. This is also why nothing that requires ongoing order — a habit, a codebase, an organization — stays ordered without continuous energy input; entropy is the default, and structure is always work against a gradient.
