# Survivorship Bias: The Tale of Forgotten Failures

Survivorship bias is the error of judging a process by only its successes — mistaking the visible winners for the full, representative sample. There are thousands of failures for every celebrated success, but failure stories rarely get told, so repeated exposure to success stories quietly resets our sense of the base rate: a college dropout becomes a billionaire, a band gets told "guitar groups are on the way out" and becomes the best-selling act in history, and we remember Gates and the Beatles as evidence the unconventional path works, while forgetting the far larger number of people who did the same unconventional things and failed.

The canonical illustration is Abraham Wald's WWII research on aircraft armor. The Army's initial instinct was to reinforce the parts of returning planes with the most bullet holes. Wald reasoned the opposite: the returning planes were survivors — the holes they had were, by definition, ones a plane could sustain and still fly home. The far more valuable, invisible data was in the planes that didn't return, whose damage patterns (concentrated in the areas the surviving planes came back _unmarked_) revealed what was actually fatal. Overcoming survivorship bias, per Wald's method, means deliberately asking what happened to the cases that never made it into your dataset at all.

The bias is especially distorting in business writing: entrepreneurship books that study successful founders' unconventional choices (dropping out, ignoring "the rules") mistake correlation for cause, ignoring that timing, luck, family wealth, and connections predicted survival far better than the unconventional choices themselves did. Since non-survivors are typically "destroyed or hidden or removed from public view" — bankrupt companies don't get case studies written about them, and unsuccessful writers don't get invited to give talks about their rejection letters — studying only survivors doesn't just give an incomplete picture, it can actively teach the wrong lessons, because the visible winners often succeeded despite their unconventional choices rather than because of them.
