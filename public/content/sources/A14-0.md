# Blue Ocean Strategy

_Blue Ocean Strategy_ (2005; expanded 2015) by INSEAD professors W. Chan Kim and Renée Mauborgne argues that the biggest strategic wins come not from beating competitors within an existing market ("red ocean" — bloody, contested, shrinking margins) but from creating uncontested new market space ("blue ocean") where competition is temporarily irrelevant. The book's central concept is value innovation: the simultaneous pursuit of differentiation and low cost, breaking the conventional assumption that a company must choose one or the other.

The core analytical tool is the "four actions framework," which asks of any product or industry: what factors should be raised above the industry standard, what should be eliminated entirely, what should be reduced well below standard, and what should be created that the industry has never offered. Applied together, these four questions are meant to break a company out of the usual trade-off between differentiation and cost. The book's most-cited real-world example is Nintendo's Wii, which won by not competing on graphics/processing power against Sony and Microsoft at all — instead using cheaper hardware and motion controls to create an entirely new category of casual, physical gameplay, ultimately outselling both rival consoles.

The theory has drawn substantive academic criticism: reviewers note the book relies on a small number of hand-picked success stories with no control group and no accounting for blue-ocean strategies that failed, making the theory difficult to falsify; some argue its core ideas substantially overlap with Gary Hamel and C.K. Prahalad's 1996 book _Competing for the Future_; and others argue "blue ocean strategy" is less a rigorous theory than a highly effective rebranding of pre-existing strategic concepts under a sticky, marketable name. Despite the criticism, the book has sold over 3.5 million copies in 43 languages, was named a bestseller by the Wall Street Journal and BusinessWeek, and won the Thinkers50 Strategy Award for Best Business Book of its decade.
