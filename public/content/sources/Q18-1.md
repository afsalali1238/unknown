Thinking, Fast and Slow is a 2011 popular science book by the Israeli-American psychologist Daniel Kahneman. Its main thesis is a differentiation between two modes of thought: "System 1" is fast, instinctive and emotional; "System 2" is slower, more deliberative, and more logical.

The book delineates rational and non-rational motivations or triggers associated with each type of thinking process, starting with Kahneman's own research on loss aversion. From framing choices to people's tendency to replace a difficult question with one that is easy to answer, the book summarizes several decades of research to suggest that people have too much confidence in human judgement.

System 1 is prone to substituting a simpler question for a difficult one. In the "Linda problem," subjects were told about an imaginary Linda — young, single, outspoken, and intelligent, concerned with discrimination and social justice — and asked whether it was more probable that Linda is a bank teller, or a bank teller and an active feminist. The overwhelming response was "feminist bank teller," violating the laws of probability, because System 1 substituted the easier question "Is Linda a feminist?"

Kahneman introduces the concept What You See Is All There Is (WYSIATI): when the mind makes decisions, it deals primarily with Known Knowns, phenomena it has already observed, and rarely considers Known Unknowns or Unknown Unknowns. He explains that humans fail to account for complexity, and that their understanding of the world consists of a small and necessarily unrepresentative set of observations.

Kahneman also expands his Nobel-winning work on Prospect Theory: people are loss-averse, more likely to act to avert a loss than to achieve an equivalent gain, and the value placed on a change in probability depends heavily on the reference point.

The book's final section distinguishes between the "experiencing self" and the "remembering self." The remembering self does not care about the duration of an experience — it retrospectively rates it by the peak (or trough) and by how it ends. "Odd as it may seem," Kahneman writes, "I am my remembering self, and the experiencing self, who does my living, is like a stranger to me."

As of 2012 the book had sold over one million copies and was a New York Times bestseller. It won the 2012 National Academy of Sciences Best Book Award and was named one of the best books of 2011 by The New York Times Book Review, The Economist, and The Wall Street Journal.
