# Hanlon's Razor: Not Everyone is Out to Get You

Hanlon's razor states: "never attribute to malice that which can be adequately explained by neglect" — or, in its more common phrasing, by stupidity or incompetence. It's a corrective against the reflex of assuming bad intent whenever someone frustrates or inconveniences us, when the far likelier explanation is a mistake, incomplete information, or simple distraction. The razor doesn't deny genuine malice exists — it just insists that, statistically, incompetence explains far more of daily friction than deliberate harm does.

The lineage of the idea is old: Napoleon is credited with nearly the same line ("never ascribe to malice that which is adequately explained by incompetence"), and Goethe wrote in 1774 that "misunderstandings and neglect create more confusion in this world than trickery and malice." German general Kurt von Hammerstein-Equord's typology of officers — dividing them into clever/diligent, stupid/lazy, clever/lazy (fit for the highest leadership), and a warning against the stupid/diligent officer who "must not be entrusted with any responsibility because he will always cause only mischief" — is used as a related illustration of judging by competence rather than assumed motive.

The article situates Hanlon's razor inside a broader "latticework" of mental models: the availability heuristic makes vivid, memorable instances of others' mistakes feel like deliberate patterns; confirmation bias causes us to selectively notice "evidence" of malice once we've already decided someone is against us; and bias from disliking/hating means we're quickest to assume malice from people or institutions we already resent. The piece closes candidly on the limit of the heuristic — the default should be to assume no malice and extend the benefit of the doubt, but once malice is genuinely confirmed by evidence, that default should reverse.
