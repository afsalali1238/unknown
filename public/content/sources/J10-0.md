# Inversion: The Power of Avoiding Stupidity

Charlie Munger is famous for saying "all I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there" — a piece of dark humor that encodes a serious mental technique borrowed from mathematician Carl Gustav Jacobi, who solved difficult problems with the maxim "man muss immer umkehren": invert, always invert. Many hard problems, Jacobi understood, are best solved backward rather than forward.

Applied outside mathematics: if you want to improve innovation at your organization, thinking forward means listing everything you could do to foster it. Thinking by inversion means listing everything that would actively discourage innovation — and then checking how many of those things your organization is already doing. Or: instead of asking what makes a good life, ask what set of choices would reliably produce a miserable one, and then simply avoid those. Inversion routinely surfaces hidden assumptions that forward-only thinking never uncovers, because "many problems," as Munger put it, "can't be solved forward."

The deeper insight is that thinking forward and thinking backward aren't symmetric — forward thinking is additive (it adds actions, which always carries some risk of unintended harm, or iatrogenics), while inversion is subtractive (removing known bad options carries much less risk of making things worse). This is why avoiding stupidity is easier, and often more valuable, than seeking brilliance: it's a much lower-risk way to improve outcomes. Inversion won't necessarily hand you the solution to a problem, but it reliably helps you sidestep the obvious traps other people keep falling into — and most of the gap between good and bad outcomes in life comes down to avoiding a small number of very avoidable mistakes.
