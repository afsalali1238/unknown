# Bill Ackman Versus Herbalife: What Happened?

For five years, Bill Ackman, founder of Pershing Square Capital, was portrayed as a crusader against Herbalife, which he called a *pyramid scheme* disguised as a legal multilevel marketing (MLM) company. Ackman built a short position of about $1 billion.

Billionaire Carl Icahn took the opposite side, buying a 13% stake (later 21%) in Herbalife, becoming its largest shareholder and attempting to engineer a *short squeeze* — driving the price up so that short sellers like Ackman would be forced to buy back shares at a loss to cover their positions. Five years later, Icahn claimed roughly $1 billion in gains; Ackman reportedly lost about $760 million on the short.

The core dispute: is Herbalife a pyramid scheme or a legal MLM? Per the SEC, pyramid schemes typically (1) promise high returns quickly, (2) don't sell a real product, and (3) emphasize recruiting over sales. Herbalife's structure had distributors recruit new distributors below them, with commissions flowing upward based on downline size and purchases rather than end-customer sales — Ackman argued this made it "the most well-run pyramid scheme in the world." Herbalife eventually settled with the FTC for $200 million and agreed to restructure its business model, while continuing to deny it was a pyramid scheme.

The distinction between a pyramid scheme and a legal MLM, per the piece: a pyramid scheme moves money up the pyramid using recruitment as the product; an MLM is a marketing strategy built around a real product, with commissions tied to actual sales and support flowing back down to distributors.
