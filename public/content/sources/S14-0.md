Humans will still want to work — to create something, share it, and be recognized for it — even if AI removes labor. Dan is less worried about industrial/factory work being automated and much more concerned with creative work: will money become obsolete, will AGI write all prose, and if struggle/status/curiosity generate meaning and AGI removes the need for them, what's left? "In a world without scarcity, how do you become the scarce good?"

## I – The meaning crisis makes meaning worth paying for

Reality develops toward greater complexity (Letter → Word → Sentence → Paragraph; Atom → Cell → Molecule → Organism). Societies evolve along two parallel tracks: their techno-economic base (Foraging → Horticultural → Agrarian → Industrial → Informational → next) and their dominant worldview (Premodern → Modern → Postmodern → next/"Metamodern"). Premodern meaning was given by a higher power; the Industrial Age's modern worldview replaced tradition with reason/science, but also over-emphasized productivity, disconnected people from tribe/community, replaced spiritual frameworks with mechanism, and outsourced agency to institutions (9-5 jobs replacing artisans).

Postmodernism's key insight was aperspectivalism — no perspective is privileged, all views are situated and partial. But this accelerated the meaning crisis further, and contains a contradiction: claiming "all perspectives are equally valid" is itself a value ranking. Some perspectives are, in fact, better.

Meaning has evolved: "Up There" (the Gods) → "Out There" (productivity/progress) → "Nowhere" (deconstructed equality) → next, possibly "In Here." This matters because taste (discerning better from worse) and agency are becoming core survival skills, and your individual perspective may be the one thing AGI can't replace.

## II – The acceleration of AI (and meaninglessness)

Referencing David Shapiro's Post Labor Economics: once AI/robots cross the "better, faster, cheaper, safer" threshold for a job, it becomes economically irrational (not immoral) to keep humans doing it — the same pattern as the hoe replacing the digging stick, the plow replacing the hoe. But if everyone loses wage income, the economy collapses, since households only get money three ways: **wages**, **transfers** (UBI, government), or **capital income** (owned assets). Transfers alone create instability; the more durable answer may be broadening capital ownership so "someday there will be almost 7B companies" (paraphrasing Naval).

Jobs likely to persist regardless of automation: high-liability roles, statutory (legally required human) positions, the experience economy (bartenders, boutiques, art), meaning-makers, and relationship/trust jobs (sales, diplomacy, negotiation). Dan focuses on the last two for creatives.

## III – The evolution and anatomy of meaning (robots vs humans)

> The elegance of the future is not in man versus machine but in their division of labor: silicon sanding the rough edges of necessity so carbon can ascend to meaning... — Chris Paik

Meaninglessness stems from stagnation (doing nothing) and isolation (no tribe). The two pillars of meaning are **progress** (forward movement) and **contribution** (connection to something greater) — both used to be outsourced (employer gave progress, religion gave connection), and now both are back in your own hands.

Three generators of meaning, forming a story:

- **Struggle** — the engine of progress; what you choose to struggle for is your purpose.
- **Curiosity** — the direction of progress; non-linear attention toward problems.
- **Status** — the proof of contribution; recognition that completes the loop.

Humans pay a premium for story because the brain is a story engine — we hate friction in utility (slow DMV lines, wrong fast-food orders) but pay top dollar to fly across the world for a five-star meal or a gripping play. Leave speed/accuracy/utility to machines; humans crave the potential for failure, the story, the myth.

## IV – The creator economy = the meaning economy

If not paid by a job, you get paid by people who believe in what you're doing. Attention becomes the scarce resource creatives compete for — but the creator economy is not winner-take-all; plenty of people with small followings live well without grinding or chasing billionaire status (Justin Welsh is cited as an example of a deliberately "quiet" creator career).

On AI slop: once everyone can produce content instantly, that content becomes instantly worthless — slop lives on a spectrum, and content that goes genuinely viral usually isn't slop by definition. Skills (marketing, writing, code, art) are being "abstracted up a layer" — the manual execution still matters less than the mind directing it.

## V – The last defensible moat is you

**The Swap Test:** if you could swap the creator and the creation would be just as valuable, AI can replace it. If the creation only works because _you_ made it — carrying your perspective, situation, and taste — that's your edge. AI cannot think from your perspective (only about it or in its style), cannot replicate your "energy signature," cannot perform genuine sensemaking, has no trajectory or stakes of death, and has no evolving taste that disagrees with its past self. By the time AI copies you, you've already moved on — "what is considered great work is a moving target."

## VI – The post-labor skill stack

1. **Agency** — the meta-skill that creates a story worth telling; the ability to act without permission, practiced through struggle, status, and curiosity.
2. **Taste** — the skill of discernment. AI creates an "infinite library problem" (a library containing every possible text is useless without curation) and an "infinite monkey problem" (random output without intention is meaningless). Curation is a more important skill than creation.
3. **Perspective** — increasing your human capacity via developmental growth (less conformist, more able to hold complexity without labeling it "wrong" out of a lack of understanding). Around half the population, per Dan, sits in a highly conformist developmental stage. Growth comes from tolerating disorientation rather than resolving dissonance too quickly (or faking a "false transformation" — performing a higher stage without embodying it).
4. **Persuasion** — the ability to get people to care about what you've made; art must merge with business, because attention mechanisms (marketing, sales, social media) determine whether anyone ever sees the work.
5. **Technical know-how** — using AI and tools; not anti-AI, but AI should offload tasks that don't need a personal touch so your mind stays free for what does.
