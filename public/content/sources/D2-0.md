# The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville

This 1984 speech, given by Warren Buffett to mark the 50th anniversary of Benjamin Graham and David Dodd's *Security Analysis*, is Buffett's direct rebuttal to the Efficient Market Hypothesis. Academic finance argued that no investor could beat the market consistently except by luck — that any apparent outperformance was equivalent to a coin-flipper on a lucky streak.

Buffett's counterargument: imagine 225 million Americans flip coins in a national contest. After 20 straight flips, about 215 people will have called it right every time purely by chance — the same result you'd get from 225 million orangutans. But if 40 of those 215 winners all came from the same zoo in Omaha, you'd stop calling it chance and start asking what they were being fed. Buffett then presents nine real, audited investment track records — Walter Schloss, Tom Knapp (Tweedy, Browne), Buffett Partnership itself, Bill Ruane's Sequoia Fund, Charlie Munger, Rick Guerin, Stan Perlmeter, and two pension funds — all of whom studied under Benjamin Graham at Columbia or worked at Graham-Newman, and all of whom beat the S&P 500 by wide margins over decades, often 15-25 percentage points a year, using completely different, uncorrelated stock picks.

The unifying method wasn't a shared stock list — their portfolios barely overlapped — but a shared intellectual approach: buying pieces of businesses for meaningfully less than their calculated intrinsic value, with a margin of safety, and ignoring price/volume patterns, beta, market timing, and the day of the week. Buffett's famous formulation: "If you buy a dollar bill for 60 cents, it's riskier than if you buy a dollar bill for 40 cents, but the expectation of reward is greater in the latter case. The greater the potential for reward in the value portfolio, the less risk there is" — directly inverting the academic definition of risk (price volatility, or beta) in favor of risk defined as the odds of permanent capital loss relative to what you paid.
