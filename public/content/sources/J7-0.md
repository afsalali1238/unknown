# Understanding the Limitations of Maps

Maps are flawed but useful — they let us borrow the experience of others to navigate unfamiliar territory, but only if we respect their limitations. The piece frames three things worth interrogating in any map: the perspective it takes, the author who drew it, and the territory it claims to represent. On perspective, maps are abstractions that necessarily lose information to save space — a one-page map of the solar system helps you learn planet order but conveys nothing of the actual vastness of space (Bill Bryson notes that on a truly-to-scale solar system map with Earth the size of a pea, Pluto would be a mile and a half away and invisibly small). Even something as basic as the standard Mercator map systematically distorts shape and scale, making most people underestimate Africa's true size by roughly 14x relative to Greenland.

On authorship: maps carry the biases and agendas of whoever drew them, whether or not that's visible on the page. The 1916 Sykes-Picot line dividing the Middle East between British and French interests is the piece's central example — a border that reflected European diplomatic convenience, not the region's actual cultures or geography, and one still driving conflict a century later. Every map — even a maximally "objective" one — reflects decisions about what to include and exclude (the piece notes Google Street View deliberately excludes identifiable people, a privacy-motivated omission that's also an editorial choice).

On territory: the fix for any single map's blind spots is to consult multiple maps of the same territory from different angles — sewer maps, transit maps, and historical maps of a city each reveal something the others miss. The piece's clearest illustration is a naval history puzzle: a map of American bases in 1947 vs. 1937 shows a huge jump, which looks like wartime expansion — but a parallel map of British bases from the same years reveals the real story, the 1940 "Destroyers for Bases" deal where Britain traded basing rights across its empire for fifty American warships it desperately needed. One map alone tells a misleading story; overlapping maps reveal the actual mechanism. The takeaway isn't to distrust maps, but to actively ask what's been simplified away, who drew this and why, and whether a second map of the same ground would tell a different story.
