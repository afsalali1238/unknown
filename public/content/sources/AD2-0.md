> Archived 2026-07-17 for personal offline reading.

Raichle, a neurologist at Washington University School of Medicine in St. Louis, noticed a recurring oddity across years of PET brain-imaging studies: a consistent set of brain regions decreased their activity the moment a subject began any externally focused task — and the same regions ramped back up the instant the task ended, regardless of what the task actually was.

## The experiment and the naming

Rather than treating this as noise to filter out, Raichle asked subjects to simply close their eyes and let their minds wander, then measured what happened. The same set of regions — spanning medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, and lateral parietal areas — lit up strongly, consuming a disproportionate share of the brain's total energy budget (commonly cited around 20%) for a brain that was, on the surface, doing "nothing." Raichle's 2001 PNAS paper coined the term "default mode" for this baseline pattern of activity.

## From pattern to network

Two years later, a Stanford team led by Michael Greicius used higher-resolution imaging to show these regions weren't just coincidentally active together — their activity was genuinely synchronized, functioning as a coordinated network rather than a set of unrelated hot spots. That team gave it the name that became standard: the default mode network (DMN).

## What it's associated with

The DMN is now linked to self-referential thought, autobiographical memory, imagining future scenarios, and thinking about other people's mental states (theory of mind) — collectively, much of what the mind does when it isn't pointed at an external task. Its activity patterns are also studied clinically: altered DMN connectivity shows up in research on depression, Alzheimer's disease, and other conditions, making it one of the more clinically significant discoveries to come out of resting-state neuroimaging.
