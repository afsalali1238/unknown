# Fooling Some of the People All of the Time: A Long Short Story (a review)

In *Fooling Some of the People All of the Time: A Long Short Story*, David Einhorn provides a detailed account of his short sale of Allied Capital equity and his subsequent attempts to expose alleged fraud by Allied management and its subsidiary, Business Loan Express, between April 2002 and December 2007. Einhorn, co-founder of the hedge fund Greenlight Capital, charges Allied with many instances of outright fraud, including falsified business appraisals, loans to nonviable businesses, and employing a convicted felon in a key position.

During his struggle to expose corporate misdeeds, Einhorn encountered a public relations campaign aimed at discrediting his reputation, an SEC inquiry into his own conduct (which found no wrongdoing), indifferent journalists, slow-acting regulators, and the discovery that his phone records had been accessed illegally. Even his wife, a journalist at *Barron's*, lost her job during the SEC investigation into him.

For the practitioner, the book serves as a reminder of what truly comprehensive fundamental research looks like — the breadth and depth of detail Einhorn and his associates unearthed while investigating Allied should impress even the most thorough investor, and it provides rare insight into a money manager's investment process.

As of 30 April 2009, Allied stock was trading near $2.46 a share, down approximately 90.5% since 15 May 2002, when Einhorn first publicly discussed his short sale — compared to a 20.0% decline in the S&P 500 over the same period. Business Loan Express filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in September 2008, and a Business Loan Express employee was sentenced to prison for fraud that November.
