# Cash Flow vs. Profit: What's the Difference?

Cash flow and profit are frequently confused, but they measure different things. Cash flow is the net balance of cash moving into and out of a business at a given point in time — it can be positive (more coming in than going out) or negative, and breaks down into operating cash flow (from normal business operations), investing cash flow (from buying or selling assets and securities), and financing cash flow (money moving between the company and its investors, owners, or creditors). Cash flow is reported on the cash flow statement, which reconciles a company's beginning and ending cash balances.

Profit, by contrast, is the balance left over after subtracting all operating expenses from revenue — reported on the income statement (profit and loss statement). Profit comes in three flavors: gross profit (revenue minus cost of goods sold), operating profit (net profit from core operations, before interest and tax — also called EBIT), and net profit (income after every expense, including interest and taxes, has been deducted).

The key distinction: profit tells you how much money is left over after expenses; cash flow tells you the actual movement of cash in and out of the bank account, independent of accounting rules about when revenue or expenses are recognized. This is why a company can be profitable on paper while having negative cash flow that threatens its ability to pay bills or make payroll — a common trap for fast-growing startups that book a large invoice as revenue before the customer has actually paid. Conversely, a company can have healthy cash flow and growing sales while failing to turn a profit, which is typical of early-stage, scaling businesses reinvesting heavily in growth. Neither metric alone tells the full story of a company's financial health — both are necessary.
