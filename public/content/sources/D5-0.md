# Howard Marks Memo Breakdown: "The Race to the Bottom"

A plain-English breakdown of Oaktree Capital co-founder Howard Marks's February 2007 memo "The Race to the Bottom," written a full year before the financial crisis broke. Marks's central idea: money itself behaves like a commodity. Since one dollar is indistinguishable from another, lenders and investors compete for deals not by improving "quality" but by cheapening money — accepting a lower interest rate, or paying a higher price — which necessarily means accepting less safety in exchange for putting more capital to work.

Marks illustrates with UK mortgage lender Abbey National raising its maximum loan-to-salary ratio from 3.5x to 5x — a direct loosening of underwriting standards driven not by borrower quality improving, but by lenders' need to deploy more capital in a "late-cycle" environment of excess optimism. He extends this to syndicated loans, where a major investment bank told him 70% of lenders in a typical deal skipped due diligence entirely, and to loan "covenants" — the contractual tripwires that let lenders act early if a borrower's finances deteriorate — which weaken whenever capital supply exceeds the demand for it, since borrowers gain negotiating leverage. Marks invokes Gresham's Law adapted to investing: bad (undisciplined) investors drive out good ones, because disciplined lenders who insist on strong covenants simply lose deals to competitors willing to accept weaker ones, forcing a race to the bottom industry-wide.

He closes with Buffett's maxim — "the less prudence with which others conduct their affairs, the greater the prudence with which we should conduct our own" — and his own conclusion, later vindicated by 2008: "the bigger the boom... the greater the bust."
