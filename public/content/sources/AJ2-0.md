> Originally developed by Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher at RAND, 1950; framing by Albert Tucker, 1950; this note summarizes https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/prisoner-dilemma/
> Archived 2026-07-17 for personal offline reading.

Flood and Dresher, both researchers at the RAND Corporation, devised the underlying game in early 1950 as part of RAND's Cold War-era interest in strategic decision-making. They ran an actual experiment, recruiting economist Armen Alchian and mathematician John Williams to play the game against each other a hundred times in succession, and observed that the two frequently chose to cooperate rather than defect — a result that puzzled them, since pure game-theoretic rationality (as understood at the time) predicted defection.

## The framing

The version now universally known — two prisoners interrogated separately, each offered a lighter sentence for betraying the other — was not Flood and Dresher's original framing but a narrative device added shortly afterward by mathematician Albert Tucker, who wanted a more vivid and accessible way to present the underlying game to an audience of Stanford psychologists in a 1950 lecture, "A Two-Person Dilemma."

## The logical structure

In the classic setup, each of two prisoners must choose to stay silent (cooperate with each other) or betray the other (defect), without knowing what the other will choose. If both stay silent, both get a light sentence. If one betrays and the other stays silent, the betrayer goes free while the silent one gets the harshest sentence. If both betray, both get a moderate sentence — worse than mutual cooperation but better than being the lone cooperator. Because betrayal is the better individual choice regardless of what the other player does, rational self-interest pushes both players toward mutual betrayal, even though mutual cooperation would have left both of them better off.

## Why it became foundational

The prisoner's dilemma became the canonical illustration of a broader class of situations — arms races, price wars, environmental commons problems, and everyday trust breakdowns — where individually rational choices produce collectively worse outcomes than cooperation would. Later work, especially Robert Axelrod's 1980s tournaments on the repeated (iterated) version of the game, showed that when the game is played repeatedly rather than once, cooperative strategies like "tit-for-tat" can outperform pure defection — anticipating exactly the kind of real-world cooperation Flood and Dresher had originally observed in their 1950 experiment.
