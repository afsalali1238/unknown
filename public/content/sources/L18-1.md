Edward Oakley Thorp (born August 14, 1932) is an American mathematics professor, author, hedge fund manager, and blackjack researcher. He pioneered the modern applications of probability theory, including the harnessing of very small correlations for reliable financial gain.

Thorp is the author of Beat the Dealer, which mathematically proved that the house advantage in blackjack could be overcome by card counting. He also developed and applied effective hedge fund techniques in the financial markets, and collaborated with Claude Shannon in creating the first wearable computer.

Thorp used the IBM 704 as a research tool to investigate the probabilities of winning while developing his blackjack game theory, which was based on the Kelly criterion, which he learned about from the 1956 paper by Kelly. He learned Fortran to program the equations needed for his theoretical research model on the probabilities of winning at blackjack.

Thorp decided to test his theory in practice in Reno, Lake Tahoe, and Las Vegas. He started his applied research using $10,000, with Manny Kimmel, a wealthy professional gambler, providing venture capital. The experimental results proved successful — he won $11,000 in a single weekend. As a countermeasure, casinos began shuffling long before the end of the deck. News spread quickly, and due to demand he wrote Beat the Dealer in 1962 (substantially updated 1966), widely considered the original guide to card counting, which sold over 700,000 copies and made the New York Times bestseller list.

While a professor at MIT, Thorp met Claude Shannon and took him and his wife on weekend trips to Las Vegas to play roulette and blackjack. Their team's roulette play was the first instance of using a wearable computer in a casino — co-developed with Shannon between 1960 and 1961, relying on one operator watching the wheel and inputting cadence via toe switches while another received a hidden earpiece signal.

Since the late 1960s, Thorp has used his knowledge of probability and statistics in the stock market, discovering and exploiting pricing anomalies in the securities markets. His first hedge fund was Princeton/Newport Partners, running from 1969 to 1989, based on market-neutral derivatives hedging. In May 1998, Thorp reported that his personal investments yielded an annualized 20 percent rate of return averaged over 28.5 years.

His second hedge fund, Ridgeline Partners, ran from August 1994 through September 2002 based on statistical arbitrage.

In 1991, Thorp was an early skeptic of Bernie Madoff's supposedly stellar investing returns, which were proved fraudulent in 2008.

Selected works: Beat the Dealer: A Winning Strategy for the Game of Twenty-One (1964); Beat the Market: A Scientific Stock Market System (1967, with Sheen T. Kassouf); The Kelly Capital Growth Investment Criterion: Theory and Practice (2011); A Man for All Markets: From Las Vegas to Wall Street, How I Beat the Dealer and the Market (2017, autobiography).
