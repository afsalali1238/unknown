These past few weeks, I've felt completely brain-fried — thinking about nothing and everything at the same time, unable to write or come up with a great idea no matter how hard I try. This letter is a guide back to a creative state, a demonstration that anyone can enter it (not just "creative people"), and a 7-day protocol to go from brain-fried to alive.

## I – You don't have ideas because there's too much interference

"I'm not a creative person" treats creativity like a talent, but at its core it's a state of consciousness everyone has — one that gets buried. Three Narrowers of the Mind:

1. **Conditioning is the enemy of wonder.** Children see the world with fresh eyes because they haven't yet internalized "you must go to school, get a job." By 20, most people think, act, and believe the same way — going down the life path assigned to them.
2. **Productivity as a priority is a losing game.** Industrialization made productivity the highest value, turning everyone into a specialist who understands one piece of the puzzle. A stressed mind only worries about survival and can't see new connections. Creativity demands useless wandering and true boredom.
3. **Infinite input and zero processing time.** Your mental metabolism can only go so fast. Creativity is rarely an input problem — you can only cook with what's in the fridge, and most people's fridges are overflowing with junk.

## II – You're not bored, you're overstimulated

Being chronically overstimulated and overcaffeinated gets mistaken for boredom. True boredom:

1. **Provides a gateway to novelty** — activates breakthrough insight when the rational mind stops trying to solve everything, and sets the scene for three flow triggers: deep embodiment, novelty, unpredictability.
2. **Reverses the hedonic treadmill** — depriving yourself of stimulation makes simple pleasures enjoyable again (Buddhist "beginner's mind").
3. **Replaces motivation with clarity.** Boredom creates space for sensemaking — the brain processes roughly 50 bits of information per second through conscious attention, and deprivation forces confrontation with suppressed problems.

## III – The 7-day protocol to slow down (how to feel alive again)

- **Day 1–2: Reduce the input fast.** Limit work to 4 hours/day. Cut your most mindless input source (podcast, scroll, news) and replace with silence. Go on a walk with no headphones, no phone.
- **Day 3–4: Digest what's already there.** Read one chapter slowly, stopping whenever a sentence makes you think. Sit with nothing for 10 minutes — no meditation app. Keep walking, actually noticing things.
- **Day 5–6: Become interested in life again.** Resist taking notes on everything — trust that important ideas return. Have one real, unhurried conversation. Extend the walk.
- **Day 7: Create with what's emerged.** Make something with no plan and no rules — write, draw, record a voice note. Don't share it yet; separate generative thinking from evaluative thinking.

## IV – To be creative, you need something to create

The reticular activating system filters millions of inputs and surfaces what you've unconsciously internalized as "this matters." You need a meaningful project — something unsolved for you, something that genuinely matters to you (not just "looks good on paper"), and something shareable (it has to take form in reality). Without a project, your mind is a boat drifting in open water; the 7-day protocol calms the storm, but you still need a direction.

To find that project: think deeply about the meaningless projects and activities you currently tolerate — cultivating awareness of what you don't want starts to create the frame of what you do.
