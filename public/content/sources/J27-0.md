# Inversion: The Power of Avoiding Stupidity

Inversion means solving a problem backward instead of forward — a technique Charlie Munger borrowed from mathematician Carl Gustav Jacobi's motto "man muss immer umkehren" ("invert, always invert"), and popularized through his own line: "all I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." The insight is that many hard problems resist a direct, forward-looking approach but become far more tractable once you flip the question: instead of asking "what makes a good life?", ask "what would guarantee misery?" and then avoid those things; instead of asking "how do we boost innovation?", ask "what specific practices would kill innovation?" — and eliminate those.

The reason inversion works as a discipline is captured in the distinction between additive and subtractive action. Thinking forward tends to add new interventions to solve a problem, which — however well-intentioned — increases your exposure to unintended harm (a concept the piece calls iatrogenics, harm caused by the attempted cure itself). Thinking backward, by contrast, is subtractive: you're identifying and removing specific sources of failure rather than layering on new, unproven fixes, which structurally carries less risk of making things worse.

The piece's closing framing is deliberately modest: inversion won't hand you the positive answer to "how do I succeed," but it reliably surfaces what to stop doing — and avoiding obvious stupidity turns out to be a far more achievable and lower-risk goal than chasing brilliance. Because most people don't naturally think backward, deliberately forcing the inverted question is itself the skill — not a one-time trick, but a habit of always checking a problem from both directions before settling on an answer.
