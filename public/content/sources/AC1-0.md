Kurt Gödel published his incompleteness theorems in 1931, at age 25, in a paper titled "On Formally Undecidable Propositions of Principia Mathematica and Related Systems." The result landed in the middle of an active program (led by David Hilbert) to place all of mathematics on a single complete, consistent, and mechanically verifiable foundation. Gödel's paper effectively proved that program could never fully succeed.

## First incompleteness theorem

For any consistent formal system F rich enough to express basic arithmetic, there exist true statements about numbers that can be formulated in F's own language but cannot be proved or disproved using F's own axioms and rules. The system is therefore either incomplete (it can't prove everything true) or inconsistent (it can prove contradictions) — and if it's consistent, it must be incomplete.

Gödel's method for proving this was itself the striking part: he devised a way to encode statements about a formal system as numbers within that same system (now called "Gödel numbering"), which let him construct a sentence that essentially says "this statement is not provable in F." If that sentence were provable, F would be proving a falsehood; if it's true, then by definition F cannot prove it. Either way, F is incomplete.

## Second incompleteness theorem

Building on the first, Gödel showed that a consistent system F capable of basic arithmetic cannot prove its own consistency — the statement "F is consistent" is itself one of the undecidable statements. To vouch for a system's consistency, you always need to step outside it into a strictly stronger system, which then has the same problem itself, one level up.

## Why it mattered beyond math

The theorems ended Hilbert's specific formalist program of grounding all mathematics in one finite, complete, mechanically checkable system, but they did not undermine mathematics itself — mathematicians simply kept working, aware that any given system will always have true-but-unprovable statements sitting just outside its reach. The theorems have since been invoked (sometimes overextended) in philosophy of mind, discussions of artificial intelligence, and any argument about the limits of self-referential, self-justifying systems.
