# Power Laws: How Nonlinear Relationships Amplify Results

A power law describes a relationship (Y = MX^B) where changing one variable produces a disproportionate change in another, regardless of the starting quantities — unlike linear relationships (doubling a cake recipe needs double the flour), power-law relationships routinely produce diminishing returns or runaway compounding depending on the exponent's sign and size. Compounding is singled out as the one power law with a variable, unbounded exponent (Future Value = Present Value × (1+i)^n) — it's arguably the most powerful and most misunderstood force the piece covers, since a small initial input can eventually dwarf a much larger later one purely due to time in the system: the piece's example has two brothers investing the same annual amount, but the one who starts 20 years earlier and stops 20 years earlier ends up with roughly 600% more money.

Peter Thiel's application to venture capital is the piece's centerpiece: he argues the return distribution of venture investing isn't a smooth bell curve where good companies do 3-10x and bad ones do 0-1x — it's a power law where a single outlier investment can return more than the entire rest of the fund combined. This has two blunt strategic implications Thiel draws out: first, only invest in companies that could plausibly return your whole fund on their own; second, because that first rule is so restrictive, there's little room for a second rule (like broad diversification) — concentrated conviction, not spreading bets, is the logically consistent response to a power-law-distributed outcome space.

Beyond finance, the piece surveys power laws across biology (Kleiber's law: an animal's metabolic rate scales to roughly the 3/4 power of its mass, meaning larger animals are more energy-efficient per unit of body weight) and language learning (Zipf's law: as few as 135 of the most common words can make up half of all spoken language, which is why learning the top 100 words first yields far more communicative value than learning words further down the frequency list). It closes on diminishing returns as the flip side of the same coin — more isn't always better past a certain point, and the example of income and happiness (subjective well-being rising steeply up to roughly $75,000/year in family income, then flattening) is offered as the power law we most often forget applies to our own life choices, not just to businesses or physics.
