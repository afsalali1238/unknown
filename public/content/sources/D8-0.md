# Ray Dalio's Changing World Order

A summary of Ray Dalio's book *The Changing World Order: Why Nations Succeed and Fail*, in which the Bridgewater Associates founder studies 500 years of rising and falling empires to identify a repeating "Big Cycle" behind shifts in global power. Dalio tracks eight measurable drivers of an empire's strength: education, inventiveness and technology, competitiveness in global markets, economic output, share of world trade, military strength, the depth of its financial center, and the status of its currency as the world's reserve currency.

The current "American world order" formed after WWII, when the 1944 Bretton Woods agreement made the dollar the world's reserve currency — following the British pound in the 1800s and the Dutch guilder in the 1600s, both of which were eventually displaced. Dalio's Big Cycle has six recurring stages: a major conflict establishes a new leading power; new institutions and systems get built; a period of peace and prosperity follows because no rival can challenge the new order; people grow used to that prosperity and begin borrowing more than they earn, inflating a bubble and widening the wealth gap; the bubble bursts, triggering internal conflict between rich and poor and forcing central banks to print money; and finally, as the empire's internal cohesion weakens, a rising external rival challenges it, often through war, producing new winners who write the next world order.

Dalio flags three signals from recent years that convinced him a shift may be underway: governments running out of room to cut interest rates and turning to money printing instead; growing internal conflict from wealth and values gaps, visible as political populism; and rising external conflict between the incumbent power (the U.S.) and a rising one (China). His two practical takeaways for investors: when central banks print heavily, currency devalues and hard assets — stocks, gold, commodities — tend to rise in nominal terms; and understanding history is the best available guide to anticipating what a shifting cycle does next, a lesson Dalio credits with helping him profit from the 2008 crash after studying the 1920s-30s bubble-and-bust in detail beforehand.
