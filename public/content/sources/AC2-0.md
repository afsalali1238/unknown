Thomas Bayes was a Presbyterian minister and amateur mathematician who never published this result during his lifetime. After his death in 1761, his friend Richard Price found the manuscript among his papers, recognized its significance, edited it, and presented it to the Royal Society, where it was published in 1763 as "An Essay towards solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances."

## What the theorem says

Bayes' theorem gives an exact way to update the probability of a hypothesis in light of new evidence. In its common form: P(H|E) = P(E|H) × P(H) / P(E) — the probability of the hypothesis given the evidence equals the probability of the evidence given the hypothesis, times the prior probability of the hypothesis, divided by the overall probability of the evidence. The P(H) term — the "prior" — represents what you believed before seeing the new evidence at all.

## The counterintuitive part: base rates

The theorem's most practically important lesson is how much the prior probability constrains what a piece of evidence can tell you. Consider a disease that affects 1 in 10,000 people, tested by a tool that's 99% accurate. Test 10,000 random people: roughly 1 has the disease and tests positive (a true positive), while the other 9,999 healthy people generate about 100 false positives at a 1% error rate. A random positive result is therefore roughly 100 times more likely to be a false positive than a true one — despite the test being "99% accurate." This is known as neglecting the base rate, one of the most common errors in both everyday and expert probabilistic reasoning.

## Why it matters beyond statistics

Bayesian reasoning has become a general framework well beyond formal statistics — in machine learning (Bayesian inference), epistemology (how rational agents should update beliefs), medicine (diagnostic reasoning), and law (evaluating evidence). The core discipline it teaches is resisting the pull of vivid, recent evidence when it conflicts with a well-established prior, and updating proportionally rather than replacing one belief with another wholesale.
