# Seth Klarman: The Forgotten Lessons of 2008

Excerpted from an annual letter by Baupost Group's Seth Klarman, this piece lists twenty lessons that should have been permanently absorbed from the 2008 financial crisis but were, in his view, "either never learned or else were immediately forgotten by most market participants" within about a year.

Selected lessons: things that have never happened before are bound to happen eventually, so investors must always be prepared for the unexpected, including sharp downward swings worse than anything previously contemplated. Risk is not inherent in an investment — it's always relative to the price paid; extreme uncertainty can actually make securities *less* risky once it has driven prices low enough. Financial models should never be trusted blindly, since markets are governed by behavioral science, not physics — reality is always more complex than any model. Ratings agencies are "highly conflicted, unimaginative dupes" that investors should never rely on. You must be willing to buy on the way down, since there is far more volume — and far less competition — on the way down than on the way back up, meaning it is almost always better to be early than late, provided you're prepared for further markdowns. And: when a government official says a problem has been "contained," pay no attention.

Klarman then lists ten "false lessons" investors seemed to have relearned instead by late 2009: that there are no long-term lessons, ever; that bad news can always be shrugged off; that markets need not stay in sync with each other; and — perhaps most dangerously — that the government can always rescue markets or override contract law at little apparent cost, which he warns is "probably doomed to a lasting legacy of government tampering with financial markets," creating what he calls the mother of all moral hazards.
