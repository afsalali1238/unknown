# Hanlon's Razor: Not Everyone is Out to Get You

Hanlon's razor states: "never attribute to malice that which can be adequately explained by neglect" — or, in the more common phrasing, by stupidity or incompetence. It's a mental safeguard against the common tendency to assume bad intent whenever someone inconveniences or frustrates us, when the far more probable explanation is that they made a mistake, acted on incomplete information, or were simply distracted. The razor doesn't deny that real malice exists — it just insists that in ordinary daily friction, incompetence is the much better bet.

The idea has deep roots: Napoleon is credited with a nearly identical formulation ("never ascribe to malice that which is adequately explained by incompetence"), and Goethe wrote in 1774 that "misunderstandings and neglect create more confusion in this world than trickery and malice." The German general Kurt von Hammerstein-Equord's famous typology of officers — clever/lazy officers fit for top leadership, clever/diligent for staff work, and a warning that "one must beware of anyone who is stupid and diligent" — is offered as a related, practical illustration of judging competence over assumed motive.

Hanlon's razor interacts with several other biases in a "latticework" way: the availability heuristic makes us over-weight vivid, memorable instances of other people's mistakes as if they were deliberate patterns; confirmation bias makes us selectively notice "evidence" of malice once we've decided someone is out to get us; and the bias from disliking/hating means we're most likely to assume malice from people or institutions we already resent. The piece is candid about the limit of the heuristic: the default should be to assume no malice and extend forgiveness, but once malice is genuinely confirmed by evidence, that default should flip — and be dealt with accordingly.
