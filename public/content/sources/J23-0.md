# How to Use Occam's Razor Without Getting Cut

Occam's razor, also known as the law of parsimony, holds that among competing hypotheses which explain the same evidence equally well, the one requiring the fewest assumptions should be preferred. In plainer language: the simplest sufficient explanation beats a needlessly complex one, because simpler theories are easier to verify, easier to falsify, and easier to act on. Credited to 14th-century friar William of Ockham but traceable back to Aristotle ("we may assume the superiority... of the demonstration which derives from fewer postulates"), the principle recurs throughout the history of science: Einstein invoked it in developing special relativity ("everything should be made as simple as possible, but not simpler"), and Newton explicitly used it to justify keeping his laws of motion minimal.

The razor shows up practically across fields. In medicine, doctors are trained to favor the diagnosis that explains the most symptoms with the fewest underlying causes — "when you hear hoofbeats, think horses, not zebras" — both because it's usually correct and because chasing rare explanations risks over-treatment and dangerous drug interactions. In criminal justice, philosopher Jeremy Bentham's principle of penal parsimony argued punishments shouldn't cause more pain than they prevent, a version of Occam's razor applied to justify against excessive sentences — this lineage fed directly into the modern prison-reform and rehabilitation movements.

The piece is equally insistent on Occam's razor's limits. Simplicity alone isn't proof — a conclusion still needs empirical backing, and reaching for the simpler-sounding explanation without evidence is really just confirmation bias wearing a disguise (the piece cites moon-landing conspiracy theories as an example where both sides claim the "simpler" story). Physicist Lisa Randall is quoted warning that the universe is often more complicated than any of us would prefer, and particles or properties that don't seem "necessary" by simplicity standards nonetheless turn out to be real. The correct use of the razor, per author Harlan Coben's clarification cited in the piece, isn't "the simplest answer is usually right" — it's "don't stack complexity onto a theory when a simpler explanation is already sufficient." It's a tool for pruning excess, not a substitute for evidence, and works best combined with other mental models like Hanlon's razor, confirmation bias, and the availability heuristic.
