# Tit For Tat

Tit for tat is a game-theory strategy — codified by mathematical psychologist Anatol Rapoport — that turns out to be the most effective approach to repeated games of cooperation and defection. The rule is simple: cooperate on the first move, then on every subsequent move do exactly what your opponent did last time. If a single-round game is being played, defection is usually the rational move; but almost nothing in real life is a single round, and iteration changes the calculus completely.

Tit for tat formalizes an instinct most people already have about reciprocity: when someone helps us, we're inclined to help them next time; when they ignore us in need, we're unlikely to help them in return. Evolution appears to select for exactly this kind of conditional cooperation in groups, because it benefits everyone over the long run even though pure unconditional cooperation would be exploitable and pure defection would leave everyone worse off than mutual cooperation.

The refinement that matters most in practice is "tit for tat with forgiveness" — occasionally cooperating even after being defected against. Straight tit for tat has a serious failure mode: two players can get locked into a cycle of mutual retaliation that neither one can escape unless someone breaks the pattern by cooperating first, even after being wronged. Since life is an iterated, compounding game rather than a single transaction, the practical takeaway is to "go positive and go first," and to assume good faith rather than malice when someone's tit-for-tat cooperation appears to lapse — most defections are mistakes, not strategy, and forgiving them is usually what restores the cooperative equilibrium that benefits both sides.
