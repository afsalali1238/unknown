Girard's first book, published in 1961 after roughly a decade teaching French literature in the United States, began as literary criticism and became the foundation of an entire theory of human desire and social conflict that he spent the rest of his career developing (later extending it to anthropology in Violence and the Sacred, 1972).

## Triangular desire

Girard's close reading of five major novelists — Cervantes, Stendhal, Flaubert, Dostoevsky, and Proust — led him to a structural pattern he believed was disguised or denied in most everyday accounts of desire: desire is never a simple two-point relationship between a subject and an object. It is always triangular — a subject desires an object because a third party, a model or mediator, desires (or appears to desire) that same object. The protagonist doesn't want the object for its own qualities; they want it because the mediator's desire makes it seem valuable.

## Why this matters for conflict

If desire is borrowed rather than original, then two people can end up wanting the exact same specific thing — a position, a partner, a possession — purely because one person's visible desire triggered the other's, not because the object is scarce or objectively superior. Girard argued this mimetic contagion, unchecked, is a primary engine of rivalry and conflict, a thread he later extended into a full anthropological theory of scapegoating and ritual violence as a social mechanism for discharging mimetic tension.

## Influence

Mimetic theory has had unusually wide influence outside literary criticism — into anthropology, theology (Girard was a practicing Catholic and read the theory through a Christian lens in later work), and more recently into technology and venture capital circles (notably through Peter Thiel, a Girard student), where it's applied to explain status competition, hype cycles, and imitation-driven market behavior.
