# 7 Negotiation Strategies CEOs Must Know

When you're the CEO, nearly every conversation is functionally a negotiation — with employees, competitors, board members, and customers, each of whom wants something you may or may not want to give, and vice versa. This piece lays out seven strategies for approaching those conversations without losing your footing.

Leverage emotional intelligence: the CEOs people most want to do business with are, unsurprisingly, the ones others simply like — complex negotiation tactics only work on top of basic human rapport. Create trust by showing some of your cards rather than treating negotiation like poker; total opacity breeds the skepticism it's trying to avoid, whereas openness about objectives and roadblocks helps the other side see a workable path to a good outcome for both sides. Plan ahead with a clear objective before sitting down, and use that objective as the guiding star through the inevitable back-and-forth. Dig deeper than the surface ask — a negotiating counterpart is not an enemy to be beaten but a person representing something important to their own organization, so staying curious about what they actually need produces better outcomes than a pure win/lose framing.

Nurture relationships continuously, not just at the negotiating table — deals close faster and more favorably when the other side already knows what kind of leader and person you are outside the transaction itself. Acknowledge your own limitations rather than letting ego block you from asking clarifying questions or bringing in outside expertise you don't have in-house. And separate practical needs (the things you must resolve now) from emotional needs (the things you want but don't strictly need immediately) — conflating the two is what causes CEOs to accept bad deals under time pressure or emotional attachment. The piece's closing advice: if the right deal isn't at this particular table, be willing to stand up, shake hands, and walk away — there will always be another opportunity.
