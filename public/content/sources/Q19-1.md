1 — THE HABIT LOOP: How Habits Work

In the fall of 1993, a woman who would upend much of what we know about habits walked into a laboratory in San Diego. For years, researchers had been trying to understand why habits exist at all — why the brain devotes so much of its limited space to storing routine behaviors, when it might seem more efficient to think through every decision fresh each time.

The answer researchers eventually settled on is efficiency. A habit is a mental shortcut: once a behavior becomes automatic, the brain can stop fully participating in decision-making and divert that focus to other tasks. This is why it's so hard to override — the brain has essentially stopped taking part in decision-making. Unless you deliberately fight it, the pattern will unfold automatically.

Habits, neurologists say, emerge because the brain is constantly looking for ways to save effort. Left to its own devices, the brain will try to make almost any routine into a habit, because habits allow our minds to ramp down more often. This effort-saving instinct is a huge advantage: an efficient brain requires less room, which makes for a smaller head, which makes childbirth easier, which results in fewer infant and mother deaths. An efficient brain also allows people to stop thinking constantly about basic behaviors, such as walking or choosing what to eat, so we can devote mental energy to inventing new things.

But this is also a huge problem, because a brain that stops paying attention to whether the habits it's formed are leading to good outcomes can't distinguish good ones from bad. That's why some habits are so risky: no matter how bad the outcome, the routine plays out automatically, cued by whatever triggers set it in motion in the first place.
