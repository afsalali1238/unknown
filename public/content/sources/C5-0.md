# Why Didn't the Fed See the Crisis Coming?

Barry Ritholtz highlights a April 2010 New York Times op-ed by Michael Burry — the investor later featured in Michael Lewis's *The Big Short* — titled "I Saw the Crisis Coming. Why Didn't the Fed?" The piece was a direct rebuttal to Alan Greenspan's claim, made weeks earlier, that "no one could have predicted the housing bubble... Everybody missed it, academia, the Federal Reserve, all regulators."

Burry disputed this directly, recalling that back in 2005 and 2006 he had argued forcefully in letters to clients of his fund, Scion Capital, that the mortgage market would melt down in the second half of 2007 and cause serious damage to the economy. His conviction was based on close study of regulatory filings for residential mortgage-backed securities: he tracked lenders as they progressively loosened standards, offering interest-only mortgages as early as 2003, then extending increasingly risky loans to subprime borrowers through 2004, with lenders selling the risk onward to Wall Street to be packaged into mortgage-backed securities. Burry watched for the moment lenders would offer the most reckless mortgages conceivable to the least qualified buyers — reasoning that this would mark the top of the bubble, since prices could only have risen that high by way of ever-loosening credit. When that moment arrived, he positioned accordingly.

The broader point of Burry's op-ed, and Ritholtz's framing of it, is that the crisis was not unforeseeable in principle — it was foreseeable to anyone doing the close, unglamorous work of reading actual loan-level data rather than trusting aggregate models and official reassurances. The Fed's failure was not a failure of information but a failure of attention and incentive.
