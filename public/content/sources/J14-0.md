# Decision Journal: Template and Example Included

We can't trust our own memories of past decisions, not because we forget, but because our brains actively edit the past to make us look smarter and more prescient than we actually were — successes get retroactively attributed to foresight, failures get retroactively assigned doubts we never actually felt at the time. Since a career is really just a long series of compounding decisions, this quiet self-editing means we never really learn from the specific reasoning that produced good or bad outcomes, which helps explain why smart people keep repeating the same mistakes. Organizations don't fix this either — they tend to treat judgment as a fixed trait to screen for in hiring, rather than a skill to develop through feedback, so if you want to close your own blind spots you have to build the feedback loop yourself.

A decision journal is that feedback loop: before you act on a consequential decision, you write down the situation, the problem as you're framing it, the variables at play, the alternatives you seriously considered (and why you rejected them), the range of possible outcomes, your actual probability estimates for each, and even mundane context like the time of day and your physical/mental state. The value isn't in the writing itself so much as what it exposes — thoughts that feel crystal-clear in your head often turn out to be genuinely murky once you try to commit them to paper, and the journal becomes a mirror that shows your thinking _before_ your brain gets a chance to polish the story afterward.

The core payoff is defeating hindsight bias, which makes past decisions look more obviously correct or incorrect in retrospect than they actually were at decision time — the journal is a written record of exactly what you knew and believed before the outcome was known, with nowhere to hide from your own handwriting. The piece also stresses precision of language: vague terms give you wiggle room to reinterpret a bad call after the fact, so the discipline is to write decisions an eight-year-old could understand. The deeper insight is that keeping a decision journal reveals you're living two parallel narratives — the flattering story your brain tells about your judgment, and the messier reality the journal captures — and the gap between those two stories is where the actual learning happens.
